# Iridian Proclitic Adverbs and Tagalog Enclitics: A Comparative Note

## Structural parallel

The Iridian proclitic adverb system and the Tagalog enclitic particle system
belong to the same typological family: both consist of a closed set of
uninflected modal/evidential particles that appear in a fixed, semantically
determined order in the vicinity of the predicate, and both cover nearly
identical semantic territory (aspect completion, continuation, polar questions,
additivity, affirmation, reportative evidentiality, exclusion, contrast,
antession, inferential evidentiality, mirativity, consequentiality, speculation,
and optative modality).

The primary structural difference is **position relative to the predicate**:

| Language | Clitic type | Position |
|---|---|---|
| Tagalog | Enclitic (second-position / Wackernagel) | **After** the first constituent of the clause |
| Iridian | Proclitic (pre-predicate) | **Before** the predicate, after all NP arguments |

In Tagalog the particles migrate to the earliest available position in the
clause regardless of where the verb sits; in Iridian the particles are anchored
to the left edge of the predicate and therefore move with the predicate if the
predicate moves. In both languages the relative order among the particles
themselves is fixed.

A secondary difference is **ordering direction**. When listed by proximity to
the predicate, the hierarchies are effectively mirror images:

| Closest to predicate | → | Farthest from predicate |
|---|---|---|
| **Tagalog** `na/pa/ba/nga` | `din/naman/raw/lang/muna` | `pala/yata/kaya/sana` |
| **Iridian** Class 1 `že/po` | Class 2 `lí/može/što…` | Class 3 `oče/izdy/nadě…` |

Tagalog's aspectual/question markers sit closest to (or first after) the topic;
Iridian's sit closest to the verb. The evidential/speculative particles are
outermost in both. The semantic ranking is the same; only the syntactic
orientation differs.

## Equivalence table

| Iridian | Gloss | Tagalog | Tagalog gloss | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `že` | PFV / already | `na` | already / now | Near-perfect correspondence. Both mark completion of a state change or action. Both extend to "no more/no longer" under negation. `na` is also grammatical with non-verbal predicates, exactly as `že` is. |
| `po` | IPFV / still, yet | `pa` | still, yet, more, else | Near-perfect correspondence. Both mark continuation of a state. Both mean "else" in wh-questions (*who else*). Both extend to "not yet" under negation. Tagalog `pa` never co-occurs with `na` in the same cluster (like Iridian `po`/`že`). |
| `lí` | Q | `ba` | [polar question] | Near-perfect correspondence. Both are pure polar question markers with no propositional content of their own. |
| `može` | ADD / also, too | `din` / `rin` | also, too, either | Near-perfect correspondence. Both mean "also" (affirmative) or "either" (negative). Both have an idiomatic "finally/at last" reading with perfective aspect (`Já može že vednik!` ≈ `Nakita mo na rin!`). `din`/`rin` is an allomorphic pair conditioned by the final consonant/vowel of the preceding word; no such allomorphy in Iridian. |
| `što` | AFF / indeed | `nga` | indeed, really, truly | Strong correspondence. Both express confirmation, affirmation, or emphasis. Tagalog `nga` is additionally used to soften imperatives ("please"), which parallels `što`'s use in imperative constructions. |
| `kamo` | REP / reportedly | `raw` / `daw` | reportedly, allegedly, supposedly | Strong correspondence. Both are reportative-evidential particles attributing the proposition to a third-party source. `raw`/`daw` is an allomorphic pair (conditioned by preceding segment); no allomorphy in `kamo`. The semantic distinction between `kamo` and the matrixless `po`-construction in the current grammar is partially analogous to Tagalog's distinction between the reportative `raw` (speaker distances from content) and a full quotative frame. |
| `sám` | EXCL / only, just | `lang` / `lamang` | only, just | Near-perfect correspondence. Both are exclusive/restrictive particles. `lang` is the colloquial reduction of `lamang`; no such colloquial reduction in `sám`. |
| `pro` | CNTR / on the other hand | `naman` | on the other hand, in turn, also (contrastive) | Strong correspondence. Both mark contrast or counter-expectation, and both extend to a polite "of course" or obviousness reading when the expected contrast is implicitly cancelled. `naman`'s additional use as a turn-sequencing marker in discourse ("my turn now") is not present in `pro` but is related to `pro`'s adversative-additive function. |
| `dal` | ANTES / before (doing X) | `muna` | first, before (doing something else), for now | Strong correspondence. Both encode antession — the action is performed prior to or in preparation for another. `muna` also means "for now/just for the moment" (temporary state), which extends `dal`'s antessive meaning into a bounded-duration reading that the 2020 grammar does not explicitly discuss for `dal`. |
| `oče` | MIR / contrary to my expectations | `pala` | so it turns out, I didn't know, (mirative) | Near-perfect correspondence. Among the closest parallels in the whole system. Both mark sudden realization, discovery, or counterexpectation. `pala` is widely cited in the typological literature on mirativity (cf. DeLancey 1997 and Anderbois 2022, cited in the 2020 grammar); the 2020 grammar cites the same literature for `oče`. |
| `izdy` | INFER (less confident) | `yata` | I think, it seems, perhaps | Partial correspondence. Both express inferential uncertainty. Tagalog `yata` is used in both declarative and interrogative sentences; Iridian `izdy` is restricted to declaratives. The speculative/question use of `yata` maps instead to `děne` (see below). |
| `hlavdy` | INFER (more confident) | `kaya` (inferential use) | so it seems, it must be | Partial correspondence. Tagalog `kaya` covers both the consequential ("therefore, that's why") and the slightly more confident inferential reading ("I suppose, it seems to follow"). The Iridian system splits these into `hlavdy` (confident inference) and `nadě` (consequential), distinguishing what Tagalog merges into one particle. |
| `nadě` | CONSQ / as a result | `kaya` (consequential use) | therefore, that's why, so | Partial correspondence (see `hlavdy` above). Iridian makes a lexical distinction that Tagalog does not. |
| `děne` | SPEC (interrogative only) | `yata` (interrogative use) | I wonder, do you think | Partial correspondence. The interrogative-only restriction on `děne` is not present in Tagalog's `yata`. The 2020 grammar's complementary distribution (`izdy`/`hlavdy` = declarative; `děne` = interrogative) is a refinement not present in Tagalog, where `yata` freely crosses that boundary. |
| `mlada` | HYP / if only, I wish | `sana` | I hope, if only, I wish | Near-perfect correspondence. Both are optative/desiderative particles expressing the speaker's wish for a counterfactual or uncertain outcome. |

## Particles without direct equivalents

### Tagalog particles with no clear Iridian counterpart

| Tagalog | Function | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `po` / `ho` | Politeness register | Tagalog's `po`/`ho` mark the speaker's deference toward the addressee. Iridian has no particle-based politeness register. (The accidental formal identity of Tagalog `po` and Iridian `po` is a notable collision.) |
| `man` | Concessive scalar / even, also | Tagalog `man` marks concessive addition ("even if, even though, also"). Iridian approximates this reading through combinations of `što` + `po` (emphatic additive in adversative contexts) but has no dedicated concessive particle. |
| `na naman` | Repetition / again, there you go again | The idiomatic cluster `na naman` expresses repetition with mild annoyance. Iridian approximates the annoyance reading through `po` + perfective but not repetition per se. |

### Iridian particles with no close Tagalog counterpart

| Iridian | Function | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `izdy` vs. `hlavdy` | Gradient inferential confidence | Iridian encodes a *two-degree* inferential scale (less vs. more confident); Tagalog collapses this into a single particle `yata`. The ability to use both together (`izdy hlavdy`) to produce an intermediate reading has no Tagalog parallel. |
| `děne` | Interrogative-only speculative | Iridian splits interrogative from declarative speculation at the particle level; Tagalog does not. |

## Ordering comparison

The Iridian phonological ordering principle — monosyllabic particles closer to
the host than disyllabic particles — can be observed in Tagalog as well, though
it has not been formally stated in Tagalog grammar descriptions. The core
Tagalog enclitics that appear soonest after the first element (`na`, `pa`, `ba`,
`nga`, `rin`/`din`) are all monosyllabic; the disyllabic particles (`naman`,
`muna`, `pala`, `sana`, `kaya`) tend to appear in later positions within the
cluster. This suggests the phonological shape preference may be a broader
typological tendency in verb-particle systems, not a language-specific
idiosyncrasy of Iridian.

The complete ordering formulae side by side:

**Tagalog** (second-position, post-first-constituent):
```
[First constituent] > ba > na/pa > nga > din/rin > naman > raw/daw > lang > muna > pala/yata/kaya/sana
```

**Iridian 2020** (pre-predicate):
```
[NP arguments] > [Class 3: oče, izdy, hlavdy, děne, nadě] > [Class 2₂σ: kamo, može] > [Class 2₁σ: lí, što, sám, pro, dal] > [Class 1: že / po] > [PREDICATE]
