\section{Theoretical background and analytical conventions}
\label{sec:th-background}

The analyses in Chapters~\ref{ch:simple} and~\ref{ch:complex} adopt a Minimalist
architecture in which clause structure is built by Merge, interpreted through
hierarchies of lexical and functional projections, and linearized according to
the strongly head-final character of Iridian. The goal is not to derive each
clause type from construction-specific templates, but to show how the recurrent
properties of the language follow from a stable structural spine: a predicate
domain, a small set of inflectional and operator projections, and an articulated
left periphery.

Within that architecture, the clause edge is treated cartographically. The high
field of the clause is not a single undifferentiated CP, but a sequence of
projections for topic, focus, clause typing, evidential/epistemic operators, and
speech-act marking. This is the machinery that the later sections of
Chapters~\ref{ch:simple} and~\ref{ch:complex} actually use to analyse topic
placement, wh-licensing, polar questions, complementation, and the ordering of
high particles. Information structure is therefore not an alternative organizing
principle to syntax; it is encoded in designated syntactic positions, especially
in \synhead{TopP} and \synhead{FocP}, within a broader head-final clause spine.

The morphology discussed in these chapters is interpreted in the same way as
elsewhere in the grammar: broadly within a Distributed Morphology model.
Syntactic structure is built first, and the exponence of heads and feature
bundles is determined at spellout. This matters directly for Iridian because
many contrasts that look at first glance like separate `constructions' are
better treated as alternative realisations of the same structural heads:
Neg\textsuperscript{0} surfaces as \ird{zá-}, \ird{staly}, or zero-plus-root
suppletion depending on the host available at PF; converb suffixes realise
feature bundles on non-finite heads; and case and agreement morphology spell out
configurations established in the syntax rather than replacing them.

The central descriptive claim of the two syntax chapters is therefore more
specific than a general appeal to `topic-comment structure'. Iridian clause
structure is organised by a head-final functional sequence in which the
absolutive pivot is strongly privileged for topical interpretation, but topic,
focus, polarity, evidentiality, and clause combining are all represented by
independent structural positions. The split-ergative alignment, the ordering of
particles, the placement of subordinate clauses, the distribution of wh-phrases,
and the complement system are analysed as consequences of that shared
architecture. This section sets out the main information-structural
assumptions and terminological conventions used throughout both chapters; the
clause spine itself is given in Table~\ref{tab:th-clause-spine} of
Chapter~\ref{ch:overview}, and the particle framework is analysed in detail in
§\,\ref{sec:th-particle-framework} below.

\subsection{Topic-comment structure as the primary information-structural
dimension}
\label{sec:th-topic-comment-theory}

The Iridian clause is organised around a \term{topic-comment bipartition}. The
\term{topic} --- the constituent the clause is semantically `about' --- is
realised in the absolutive (direct) case and occupies the specifier of
\synhead{TopP}, the highest argument-hosting projection in the left periphery.
The \term{comment} --- the predication made about the topic --- corresponds
broadly to the \synhead{IP}-internal material and the predicate.

Three notions must be kept distinct in this system: \term{subject},
\term{absolutive pivot}, and \term{discourse topic}. Iridian does not require a
language-wide subject relation as the primitive around which clause structure is
organised. The stable cross-clausal privilege belongs instead to the
absolutive/direct argument: it is the argument that most readily serves as the
clausal pivot for topichood, topic drop, and discourse continuation. In the
nominative-domain aspects this pivot is usually the agent or sole argument,
which creates a superficial resemblance to familiar subject-prominent systems.
In the ergative-domain aspects it is instead the patient. The term
\emph{subject} is therefore only a descriptive convenience for those local
configurations in which the absolutive pivot happens also to be the agent; it is
not the organizing primitive of Iridian syntax.

This architecture has consequences for the alignment system. The split-ergative
pattern of Iridian (perfective/retrospective aspects trigger ergative-absolutive
case marking; the nominative aspects trigger nominative-absolutive marking) is
not a syntactically arbitrary quirk: both patterns converge on placing the
discourse-central participant in the absolutive (direct) case, thereby licensing
it as the canonical clause topic. In the perfective/retrospective, the patient
is the typical pivot-topic and therefore receives the absolutive. In the
nominative aspects, the agent or sole participant is the typical pivot-topic and
receives the absolutive accordingly. The aspect system is therefore partly an
information-structural resource, allowing the speaker to select which argument
will serve as the default discourse center (see §\,\ref{sec:topic-comment} for
detailed illustration).

The ergative-domain agent is not thereby removed from the core syntax. It
remains the initiator, controller, and often the most volitional participant of
the event, but its ordinary place is inside the comment domain as a non-pivot
core argument. When specially anchored or fronted, it receives a marked
contrastive, resumptive, or topic-shift interpretation rather than the neutral
clause-topic interpretation associated with the absolutive.

The practical effects of this architecture for word order are described in
§\,\ref{sec:word-order}: the topic-initial, verb-final order is the unmarked
pattern, and any departures from it are driven by information-structural rather
than purely grammatical requirements.

\subsection{Head-finality}
\label{sec:th-head-finality}

Iridian is a rigorously head-final language. Both phrases and clauses are
internally verb-final or head-final: arguments precede their predicates,
modifiers precede their heads, adpositions follow their nominal complements (as
postpositions, cf.\ §\,\ref{sec:adpositions}), and subordinate clauses precede
the matrix predicate. This property, which is statable as a general principle of
phrase structure and applies at every level of embedding, has several major
consequences that recur throughout the syntactic analysis.

For the clause in isolation, head-finality means that the finite verb is always
clause-final in standard Iridian: it is the head of the highest verbal
projection and accordingly surfaces after all its specifiers and complements.
For clause-combining, it means that subordinate clauses precede the matrix
clause whose head they are complements of (see §\,\ref{sec:th-subordination}
below). For the particle system, it means that particles in higher projections
surface earlier in the linear string: an operator that takes scope over the full
\synhead{IP} will surface before the topic and before \synhead{IP}-internal
material, because the specifier of a high projection must precede the head of a
lower one.

Head-finality also has consequences for relativization and complementation.
Relative clauses precede the head noun they modify; finite complement clauses
precede the matrix predicate that embeds them; and purpose-clause converbs
precede the verb they are oriented toward. These properties are not stipulated
separately but follow from the single head-final phrase structure combined with
the clause-spine in Table~\ref{tab:th-clause-spine}.

\subsection{The particle framework and its integration into the syntax
chapters}
\label{sec:th-particle-framework}

The adverbial particle system, analysed in detail in §\,\ref{sec:adv-particles}
of Chapter~\ref{ch:function-words}, establishes the main empirical evidence for
the left-peripheral structure summarised in Figures~\ref{fig:th-clause-fields}
and \ref{fig:th-left-periphery}. The particles serve here as syntactic
diagnostics: their obligatory ordering relative to one another, their scope
properties, and their ability or inability to license fragment answers are all
direct reflex of the projections they occupy.

Four structural positions are distinguished in the particle system. The first
three correspond to the three adverbial particle classes of
Chapter~\ref{ch:function-words}; the fourth is \ird{by}, which occupies a
distinct structural level and is treated separately in §\,\ref{sec:by-pol-head}
and §\,\ref{sec:th-polar-questions}.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em, labelwidth=!, labelindent=0em]
\item[\upshape Clause-typing operator (\synhead{PolP}).] The interrogative
	particle \ird{by} is the overt exponent of \synhead{Pol\textsuperscript{0}},
	the head of \synhead{PolP} (§\,\ref{sec:by-pol-head}). \synhead{PolP} occupies
	level~7 in the clause spine, above \synhead{FocP} and below \synhead{TopP}. It
	is structurally and functionally distinct from the adverbial particle classes:
	it is a clause-typing projection, not a comment-domain or stance operator.
	Because it is below \synhead{TopP}, \ird{by} surfaces post-topic in the linear
	string (topic $>$ \ird{by} $>$ focus field $>$ predicate). Because
	\synhead{EvidP} and \synhead{SpeechActP} occupy higher positions in the spine,
	their particles scope over the interrogative force introduced by \ird{by} and
	surface to \ird{by}'s left.
\item[\upshape Zone~\textsc{i} (upper peripheral: evidential and speech-act).]
	Speech-act and discourse-relational particles (\ird{nadě}, \ird{mlada},
	\ird{jse}) occupy \synhead{SpeechActP}. Evidential and epistemic particles
	(\ird{oče}, \ird{hlavdy}, \ird{ktady}, \ird{či}) occupy \synhead{EvidP}.
	Both sets are structurally above \synhead{TopP} (levels~9--10 in the
	spine) and scope over the full proposition, including the topic and any
	interrogative operator in \synhead{PolP}.
\item[\upshape Zone~\textsc{ii} (comment-domain).] Operators whose scope is
	restricted to the comment domain --- \ird{och} (additive), \ird{moc}
	(affirmative), \ird{kamo}/\ird{kam} (exclusive), \ird{dlí} (interim
	sequential) --- occupy the \synhead{AspP} tier below \synhead{IP}. They
	are focus-sensitive where applicable and scope over the predicate domain,
	not the full proposition.
\item[\upshape Zone~\textsc{iii} (phasal).] The phasal particles \ird{v}
	(\trsl{already}) and \ird{s} (\trsl{still} / \trsl{not yet}) occupy
	\synhead{PhasP} immediately above \synhead{PredP}. Their scope is strictly
	predicational.
\end{description}

\noindent The surface ordering from leftmost to rightmost --- Zone~\textsc{i}
particles $>$ topic $>$ \ird{by} $>$ Zone~\textsc{ii} $>$ Zone~\textsc{iii} $>$
predicate --- is a direct consequence of the clause spine combined with
head-finality. The important analytical point is that \ird{by}'s surface
proximity to the Zone~\textsc{ii} particles does not indicate shared
constituency: \ird{by} is in \synhead{PolP} between \synhead{TopP} and
\synhead{FocP}, while Zone~\textsc{ii} items are in \synhead{AspP} below
\synhead{IP}. Cross-references from the simple and complex sentence chapters to
§\,\ref{sec:adv-particles} and §\,\ref{sec:by-pol-head} are intended to be
followed for formal motivation.


\section{The left periphery: topics, operators, and clause typing}
\label{sec:th-left-periphery}

\subsection{Topic and topic drop}
\label{sec:th-topic}

The specifier of \synhead{TopP} hosts the clause topic. In canonical
declaratives the topic is realised by the absolutive pivot of the predicate; it
surfaces clause-initially and is invariably in the direct case. Because Iridian
permits pro-drop, the topic position may be phonologically empty when the
referent is recoverable from the discourse. Topic drop is the default in
contexts of topical continuity; overt topicalization of a pronominal ---
especially with the peripheral particles --- carries contrastive or
focus-related force.

When the topic is dropped, Zone~\textsc{i} particles appear at the left edge of
the string without any overt nominal to their left. This is a phonological
consequence of null-topic, not a separate fronting operation: the particles
remain in their regular post-topic positions (§\,\ref{sec:adv-particles}).

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek zdravime.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} sleep-\Prog{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is sleeping.} \lingcontext{canonical topic-initial}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Zdravime.//
\glb sleep-\Prog{}//
\glft \trsl{[He] is sleeping.} \lingcontext{topic drop; referent established}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Oče zdravime.//
\glb \textsc{mir} sleep-\Prog{}//
\glft \trsl{Oh, [he] is sleeping!} \lingcontext{topic drop with peripheral mirative; \ird{oče} in post-topic position}//
\endgl
\xe

\subsection{Focus and focus movement}
\label{sec:th-focus}

The \synhead{FocP} position immediately below \synhead{PolP} is the target for
focus movement. In the unmarked declarative clause, focus falls in situ on the
rightmost comment-domain constituent immediately before the verb (the
`comment-final' position), consistent with the general word-order tendency
toward late-focus. A focused constituent or wh-phrase may optionally move to
\synhead{SpecFocP}, surfacing after any peripheral particles. This movement is
not syntactically obligatory; it is discourse-driven, signalling marked focus,
contrast, topicalization, or discourse prominence. The absence of overt movement
is therefore the neutral or unmarked option, especially in content questions,
where a wh-phrase may remain in situ (see §\,\ref{sec:th-wh-interrogation}).

It follows from this architecture that \synhead{SpecFocP} is not an exclusively
interrogative position: it is the target of focus movement in general. A
focalized non-wh constituent moves there in much the same way as a wh-phrase.
The functional difference --- between a focused declarative and a content
question --- comes from the clause-level interrogative operator (\ird{by} or a
phonologically null counterpart in \synhead{PolP}), not from the focus position
itself.

\subsection{The clause-typing operator \ird{by} and matrix interrogatives}
\label{sec:th-polar-questions}

Matrix interrogatives project \synhead{PolP}. Pol\textsuperscript{0} may be
spelled out overtly as \ird{by} or left phonologically null. In polar questions,
overt \ird{by} is the ordinary and unmarked explicit strategy. In content
questions, both overt \ird{by} and null Pol\textsuperscript{0} are standardly
available: a wh-question may appear with or without \ird{by}, and the choice
reflects the speaker's discourse position rather than a grammatical asymmetry
between question types. The full discussion of the overt/null alternation in
content questions is in §\,\ref{sec:th-wh-interrogation}; the present subsection
focuses on the clause-typing function of \ird{by} itself and on the properties
shared across all matrix interrogatives (§\,\ref{sec:by-pol-head}).

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek by zdravime?//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} \textsc{q} sleep-\Prog{}//
\glft \trsl{Is Marek sleeping?}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek by v ščenik?//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} \textsc{q} already arrive-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Has Janek already arrived?}//
\endgl
\xe

The interaction of \ird{by} with Zone~\textsc{i} particles (evidential and
speech-act operators) follows directly from their relative positions in the
functional hierarchy. Because those particles occupy projections above
\synhead{PolP}, they scope over the interrogative force introduced by \ird{by}:
a mirative question (\ird{oče~by}) presents the polar question as
counterexpectational; a reportative question (\ird{či~by}) presents it as
relayed from another source.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Tak tóm oče by Janku v koupnik?//
\glb this book.\Dir{} \textsc{mir} \textsc{q} Janek.\Trs{} already buy-\Trs{}-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Has Janek, surprisingly, already bought this book?}//
\endgl
\xe

A constituent may be fronted to Spec,\synhead{FocP} or set as a topic in
Spec,\synhead{TopP} inside a polar question, while \ird{by} in
Pol\textsuperscript{0} continues to type the clause as interrogative. The
fronting of a non-wh constituent inside a polar question is not a special
interrogative mechanism but ordinary information structure operating inside an
interrogative clause. A direct-case topic may remain in Spec,\synhead{TopP}; a
narrow-focus non-wh constituent may move to Spec,\synhead{FocP}; either
operation is independent of the interrogative clause typing supplied by
\ird{by}. This point is important for understanding argument and adjunct
fronting in questions and prevents conflating topic/focus movement with the
mechanism that licenses interrogative force: interrogative force belongs to
\synhead{PolP}, while focus and topic placement belong to \synhead{FocP} and
\synhead{TopP} respectively.


\section{Content questions and \textit{wh}-interrogation}
\label{sec:th-wh-interrogation}

\subsection{Licensing of content questions: \synhead{PolP} and wh-operators}
\label{sec:th-single-wh}

Content questions are licensed by the combination of an interrogative operator
in \synhead{PolP} and a wh-expression inside the clause. They are not licensed
by movement alone. \textsc{Pol}\textsuperscript{0} may be phonologically null in
neutral content questions, but it is structurally present as the clause-typing
head; the wh-operator within its scope domain is the interrogative variable that
the question is resolved against.

The distributional rule for wh-phrases is as follows. In neutral content
questions, a single wh-expression may remain in situ in its base-generated
argument or adjunct position. One wh-expression may optionally move to
Spec,\synhead{FocP} when assigned marked focus; this movement signals that the
wh-phrase is singled out against an already salient frame, or contributes
contrast, insistence, or other discourse prominence. No clause permits more than
one wh-phrase to front overtly to Spec,\synhead{FocP}.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek jena mulša?//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} where live-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Where does Marek live?} \lingcontext{neutral; wh in situ}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Jena Marek mulša?//
\glb where Marek.\Dir{} live-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Where does Marek live?} \lingcontext{marked; wh to \synhead{SpecFocP} for discourse prominence}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Tak tóm jeda koupnik?//
\glb this book.\Dir{} who buy-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Who bought this book?} \lingcontext{neutral; wh in situ}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Jeda tak tóm koupnik?//
\glb who this book.\Dir{} buy-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Who bought this book?} \lingcontext{marked; wh to SpecFocP}//
\endgl
\xe

A fronted wh-phrase does not itself license interrogative force; its movement
signals discourse prominence, contrast, or singled-out status of the
wh-variable. The interrogative clause type is established by the \synhead{PolP}
layer independently of whether any fronting has occurred. A content question is
therefore structurally defined by the conjunction of an interrogative operator
in \synhead{PolP} and a wh-expression within \synhead{IP}, not by the position
of the wh-phrase at the surface.

Matrix content questions permit both overt and null Pol\textsuperscript{0}, and
these are \emph{both} standard options, not a default with an emphatic variant.
The two differ in discourse profile. A content question with null
Pol\textsuperscript{0} is the structurally lighter form, appropriately neutral
in ordinary information-seeking contexts. A content question with overt \ird{by}
is more explicitly typed as a direct question: it is more interactionally
foregrounded, conversationally engaged, or oriented toward a specific addressee
in the current exchange. The difference is a pragmatic tendency, not a semantic
or grammatical opposition. Both forms are equally grammatical in standard
Iridian.

The same independence holds between \ird{by} and the fronting of the wh-phrase.
\ird{By} may appear in a content question regardless of whether the wh-phrase
has moved to the left-peripheral focus position or remains in situ. This gives
four available combinations: null Pol with in-situ wh, null Pol with fronted wh,
overt \ird{by} with in-situ wh, and overt \ird{by} with fronted wh. All four are
well-formed; they differ only in the independent contributions of clause-typing
explicitness (\ird{by}/null Pol) and discourse prominence (fronting/in situ).

The following paradigm illustrates all four for the content question \trsl{where
does Marek live}:

\pex\label{ex:wh-four-combinations}
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek jena mulša?//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} where live-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Where does Marek live?} \lingcontext{null Pol; wh in situ: neutral}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek jena by mulša?//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} where \textsc{q} live-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Where does Marek live?} \lingcontext{overt \ird{by}; wh in situ: explicit, interactionally foregrounded}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Jena Marek mulša?//
\glb where Marek.\Dir{} live-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Where does Marek live?} \lingcontext{null Pol; wh fronted: wh-phrase discourse-prominent}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Jena by Marek mulša?//
\glb where \textsc{q} Marek.\Dir{} live-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Where does Marek live?} \lingcontext{overt \ird{by}; wh fronted: both clause and wh-phrase foregrounded}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent The same four-way system extends to transitive and oblique content
questions. The wh-word type (agent, patient, location, instrument, and so on)
does not restrict the availability of any combination; all four remain possible
across the full inventory of wh-operators in Table~\ref{tab:nm-interrogatives}.

When Zone~\textsc{i} particles are present, they surface above the wh-phrase
(whether in situ or fronted) and above any overt \ird{by}, because they occupy
projections higher in the spine:

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Jena hlavdy Marek mulša?//
\glb where \textsc{infr} Marek.\Dir{} live-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Where would Marek presumably be living?}\\
      \lingcontext{\ird{hlavdy} in \synhead{EvidP} above \synhead{PolP}; wh fronted}//
\endgl
\xe

\subsection{Multiple \textit{wh} and in-situ licensing}
\label{sec:th-multiple-wh}

When the clause contains more than one wh-phrase, the ordinary pattern is for
all wh-phrases to remain in situ. At most one wh-phrase may front to
\synhead{SpecFocP}, and only when that phrase is assigned specially marked focus
within the pair-list interpretation. The remaining wh-phrases are interpreted as
interrogative variables in their base positions, licensed by the clause-level
interrogative operator (overt \ird{by} or a null \synhead{PolP} head).

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Jede ježe koupnik?//
\glb who what buy-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Who bought what?}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Jede jena mulša?//
\glb who where live-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Who lives where?}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent In these multiple-wh clauses both phrases remain in their
\synhead{IP}-internal base positions and are bound by the null interrogative
operator in \synhead{PolP}. The general licensing principle is: a wh-word within
the scope domain of the clause-level interrogative operator in \synhead{PolP} is
interpreted as an interrogative variable, regardless of its surface position.
Overt movement to \synhead{SpecFocP} marks the fronted phrase as the salient or
foregrounded variable in the pair-list; it does not itself establish the
interrogative type of the clause, which is always a function of \synhead{PolP}.

\subsection{Tag, echo, and indirect questions}
\label{sec:th-tag-echo}

Tag and echo questions, and indirect questions embedded under \ird{poby}, are
treated in §\,\ref{sec:questions} and §\,\ref{sec:poby} respectively. For
embedded content questions in \ird{poby}-complements, the same distributional
principle applies as in matrix clauses: the embedded wh-phrase may remain in
situ (the neutral option) or move to the left periphery of the embedded clause
for marked focus. Wh-fronting within the embedded clause's own periphery is
therefore not obligatory; it signals the same kind of discourse prominence as
fronting in matrix questions. The \ird{poby} complementizer marks the embedded
clause as a non-assertive complement regardless of whether the internal
wh-phrase has fronted. The full analysis of \ird{poby} and its complement types
is in §\,\ref{sec:poby}.

\section{Word order}\label{sec:word-order}

The traditional descriptive shorthand for Iridian word order is \term{SOV}, but
that label should not be mistaken for the structural primitive of the clause. A
more faithful characterization is \term{topic-initial, verb-final}, or more
specifically \term{absolutive-first, verb-final}. The finite verb itself is
rigidly clause-final; the ordering of preverbal nominals is governed by
information structure and alignment rather than by a fixed subject-object
template.

In nominative-domain clauses this topic-initial pattern often happens to look
like SOV, because the agent is also the absolutive pivot. In ergative-domain
clauses, however, the same template yields a patient-topic order of the type
\term{absolutive $>$ ergative $>$ verb}. The deeper generalisation is therefore
not `subject before object' but `pivot-topic before comment, with the finite
verb last'.

\subsection{Lexical manner and frequency adverbs in the preverbal field}
\label{sec:lexical-adverb-position}

Lexical adverbs belong to the comment domain of the clause rather than to the
topic field. In the unmarked structure, derived manner adverbs in \ird{-ě} are
best analysed as low adjuncts adjoined to \synhead{vP} or to the outer
\synhead{VP}. Because Iridian is head-final, these adverbs surface in the
preverbal field, below topic and focus material and outside the fixed hierarchy
of preverbal particles described in Chapter~\ref{ch:function-words}. Their
neutral placement is therefore lower than that of clause-typing or stance
particles but still to the left of the finite verb.

\begin{center}
\term{[\synhead{TopP} Topic [\synhead{IP} \ldots [\synhead{vP} MANNER-ADV [\synhead{VP} Object V ]]]]}
\end{center}

\noindent The neutral surface order in an intransitive clause is accordingly
\term{Adverb $>$ Verb}:

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Skorně zmačime.//
\glb quickly run-\Prog{}//
\glft \trsl{I am running quickly.}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent In a transitive clause, where the object remains in the low
complement domain, the neutral order is \term{Adverb $>$ Object $>$ Verb}:

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Skorně bylam piaštébime.//
\glb quickly child.\Dir{} eat-\Appl{}-\Prog{}//
\glft \trsl{I am feeding the child quickly.}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent Frequency adverbs such as \ird{ohly} \trsl{sometimes},
\ird{roca} \trsl{often}, \ird{člíně} \trsl{infrequently}, and \ird{sabo}
\trsl{never} belong to the same broad lexical-adverb domain, though their
semantics are higher and more event-quantificational than those of pure manner
adverbs. Even so, they are not members of the rigid particle series. More
leftward placement of lexical adverbs is possible under discourse pressure, but
such ordering belongs to marked information structure and should not be taken
as the neutral pattern.

\section{The copula}\label{sec:copula}

Iridian distributes equative and classificatory predication across two
constructions that differ both structurally and semantically. The default
strategy is the \term{zero copula}: a null functional head that licenses a bare
equative predication without event structure. The second strategy is the overt
marked copular verb \ird{neh-}, which introduces an event of transition and
projects a full verbal spine. The two constructions are not merely stylistic
variants; they encode a principled semantic contrast between atemporal
classification on the one hand and entering a classification on the other.

beautiful} or \trsl{be good} --- belongs to the stative verb system and is not
treated here; see Chapters~\ref{ch:overview} and \ref{ch:verbs}. Existential
predication through the \ird{jec-}/\ird{zad-} pair is structurally distinct and

\subsection{Equative and class-membership clauses: the zero copula}%
\label{sec:zero-copula}

\subsubsection{The construction and its distribution}

The default pattern for equative clauses --- those asserting identity, role, or
class membership --- is zero copula. The topic \synhead{DP} occupies the initial
position and the predicative complement appears in the direct case in
clause-final position; no overt verb is present. This is the stylistically
neutral, unmarked construction.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek doktor.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} doctor.\Dir{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek is a doctor.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Bách němscvení kompozitor.//
\glb Bach.\Dir{} German.\textsc{attr} composer.\Dir{}//
\glft \trsl{Bach is a German composer.}//
\endgl
\xe

Inclusive and class-membership predications follow the same zero-copula pattern
and carry no inference beyond simple predication. The predicative complement
always appears in the direct case regardless of which case would otherwise be
assigned in the same structural position.

\subsubsection{Structural analysis: \synhead{EquP} and the absence of AspP}

The zero copula is not merely absent from the surface string; it is the
phonologically null exponent of a dedicated functional head,
Cop\textsuperscript{0}, which projects an \term{equative phrase}
(\synhead{EquP}). \synhead{EquP} is the functional layer that licenses the
predication relation between the topic \synhead{DP} and the predicative
\synhead{DP}. Crucially, \synhead{EquP} is \emph{not} selected by
Asp\textsuperscript{0}: no aspectual projection is present in the zero-copula
clause.

The reason is structural. Aspect requires an eventuality --- a state, process,
or transition --- to scope over. Equative predications of the type \trsl{Marek
is a doctor} or \trsl{Bach is a composer} express a relation between two
individuals, not an eventuality in any aspectual sense. There is no event of
being-a-doctor that could be viewed as complete, ongoing, or prospective in the
way that eating or arriving can. In the absence of an eventuality,
\synhead{AspP} cannot be projected, and \synhead{EquP} is selected directly by
\synhead{NegP} (when present) or by the higher \synhead{IP} layer. The
affirmative clause structure is:

\begin{center}
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{EquP}
    [{\synhead{DP}\\\textit{Marek}\\{\footnotesize\textsc{[dir]}}\\{\scriptsize topic}}]
    [{Eq$'$}
      [{\synhead{DP}\\\textit{doktor}\\{\footnotesize\textsc{[dir]}}\\{\scriptsize pred.}}]
      [{Eq$^{0}$\\{$\varnothing$}}]
    ]
  ]
\end{forest}
\end{center}

The two DPs bear the direct case as the default case assigned under
\synhead{EquP}: both the topic and the predicative complement are direct-case
nominals within a caseless, event-free predication domain.

\subsubsection{Temporal restriction}

The temporal corollary of the absence of \synhead{AspP} is that the zero-copula
construction is restricted to \term{present-time} and \term{atemporal} or
\term{generic} predications. A sentence like \ird{Marek doktor} is interpreted
as holding at the moment of utterance or as a classification that transcends any
particular time. It cannot, without additional context, be interpreted as a past
or future statement. This restriction follows directly from the lack of any
tense or aspect projection: there is no functional head in the clause that could
anchor the predication to a non-present time.

For past equative predications, \ird{neh-} in the retrospective (\ird{nac}) is
used; see §\,\ref{sec:neh-past}. For future-oriented predictions, the
contemplative of \ird{neh-} (\ird{nách}) is available. The zero copula's
restriction to present and generic contexts thus parallels the zero-copula
pattern found in Russian, Arabic, and Hebrew, where the copula is similarly
absent in the present indicative but required in other tenses --- though in
Iridian the structural basis is the absence of \synhead{EquP}'s eventuality
rather than the suppression of a tensed copula.

\subsubsection{Negation: \ird{staly} as the canonical Neg\textsuperscript{0}
exponent}

When a zero-copula equative is negated, \ird{staly} appears in clause-final
position. The verbal negative prefix \ird{zá-} does not arise: it requires a
phonological host within the verbal complex, and no such host is present.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek doktor staly.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} doctor.\Dir{} \NegCop{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek is not a doctor.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Doktor Marek staly.//
\glb doctor.\Dir{} Marek.\Dir{} \NegCop{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek is not the doctor.}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent The two examples differ in which \synhead{DP} occupies the topic
position, not in the position of \ird{staly}. The semantic contrast ---
\trsl{not a doctor} (predicational) vs.\ \trsl{not the doctor}
(identificational) --- arises from topicalization, not from any change in the
negation structure.

Structurally, \ird{staly} is the phonological exponent of Neg\textsuperscript{0}
in equative contexts. It bears no aspect marking, has no paradigmatic
alternants, and cannot appear in any non-negative environment --- all properties
consistent with its status as a functional head rather than a verbal root. Its
clause-final position is not exceptional: as the head of \synhead{NegP} in a
head-final language, it follows its complement (the \synhead{EquP} predication).
\synhead{NegP} selects \synhead{EquP} directly, with no intervening
\synhead{AspP}:

\begin{center}
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{NegP}
    [{\synhead{DP}\\\textit{Marek}\\{\footnotesize\textsc{[dir]}}\\{\scriptsize topic}}]
    [{Neg$'$}
      [{Neg$^{0}$\\\ird{staly}}]
      [\synhead{EquP}
        [{\synhead{DP}\\\textit{doktor}\\{\footnotesize\textsc{[dir]}}\\{\scriptsize pred.}}]
        [{Eq$'$}
          [{Eq$^{0}$\\{$\varnothing$}}]
        ]
      ]
    ]
  ]
\end{forest}
\end{center}

\noindent The topic \synhead{DP} occupies Spec,\synhead{NegP}; the predicative
\synhead{DP} remains within \synhead{EquP} as the complement of
Eq\textsuperscript{0}.

This framing inverts the usual presentation of \ird{staly} as an `exceptional'
or `restricted' negation strategy. It is in fact the \emph{canonical} exponent
of Neg\textsuperscript{0}: it surfaces in exactly the position the clause spine
predicts for a head-final \synhead{NegP} head. The prefix \ird{zá-}, by
contrast, is the PF-conditioned variant --- it arises only when a verbal complex
is present and provides a cliticization host. When no such host exists, as in
zero-copula clauses, Neg\textsuperscript{0} surfaces freely in its default
head-final position as \ird{staly}. The same logic applies cross-linguistically:
Turkish \ird{değil} and Swahili \ird{si} occupy the same structural slot in
their respective zero-copula systems, surfacing as invariant clause-final (or
predicate-final) negative heads in the absence of a verbal host. See
§\,\ref{sec:clausal-negation} for the unified treatment across all predicate
types, and §\,\ref{sec:vm-neg-exponents} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs} for the
formal Distributed Morphology account.

When \ird{neh-} is present --- that is, when the copular predication is
expressed through the overt transitional verb rather than the zero copula ---
the construction has a verbal host, and negation therefore follows the standard
verbal pattern: \ird{zá-} procliticizes onto \ird{neh-}. \ird{Staly} does not
arise in this context not because it is `blocked' by \ird{neh-} but because the
condition for its appearance --- absence of a verbal host --- is not met.

\subsection{Identificational equatives and participant nominals}%
\label{sec:ident-equative}

When the predicative complement of a zero-copula equative clause is a
\term{participant nominal} (a nominalised verb form,
§\,\ref{sec:nmlz-predicative} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs}), the construction is
\term{identificational}: the topic \synhead{DP} is asserted to be the unique
referent satisfying the description encoded in the nominal. The schematic
contrast with a plain verbal assertion is:

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Lobek Janku piaštnik.//
\glb apple.\Dir{} Janek.\Trs{} eat-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek ate the apple.} \lingcontext{verbal clause: propositional assertion}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Lobek Jancí piaštnikou.//
\glb apple.\Dir{} Janek.\Ind{} eat-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{}//
\glft \trsl{The apple is what Janek ate.} \lingcontext{identificational equative: patient nominal}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent In (b), the apple is not merely a participant in the event but is
being identified as the unique entity satisfying the nominalised description. An
agent-nominal equative identifies the topic as the agent of the nominalised
event:

\ex
\begingl
\gla Janek lubcí piaščikou.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is the one who ate the apple.}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent All internal arguments of the nominalised event appear in the indirect
case (\Ind{}) regardless of the case they would bear in the finite verbal
clause: the agent of the transitive verb (normally \Trs{}) and the patient of
the antipassive-derived agent nominal (normally suppressed in finite contexts)
both appear as \Ind{} arguments within the nominal predicate.

\paragraph{Negation of identificational equatives.}
When an identificational equative is negated with \ird{staly}, the result is
\term{identificational negation}: the topic \synhead{DP} is denied as the unique
satisfier of the description, with the strong implication that some other
referent is the actual satisfier. This contrasts systematically with verbal
propositional negation:

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Lobek Janku zápiaštnik.//
\glb apple.\Dir{} Janek.\Trs{} \Neg{}-eat-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek did not eat the apple.} \lingcontext{propositional negation: denies that the event occurred}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Lobek Jancí piaštnikou staly.//
\glb apple.\Dir{} Janek.\Ind{} eat-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \NegCop{}//
\glft \trsl{The apple is not what Janek ate.} \lingcontext{identificational negation: event presupposed; identification denied}//
\endgl
\xe

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek lubcí piaščikou staly.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \NegCop{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is not the one who ate the apple.} \lingcontext{agent-identification denied: it was someone else}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent Example (a) of the first set is compatible with the eating not having
occurred at all. Examples using \ird{staly} presuppose that the eating did occur
and assert that the identified entity is the wrong one: \ird{piaštnikou staly}
says that the apple is misidentified as Janek's eating; \ird{piaščikou staly}
says that Janek is misidentified as the eater. In both cases the implication
that the correct referent exists --- that something else was eaten, or that
someone else ate the apple --- arises from the exhaustivity of the identificational
predication itself rather than from any special property of \ird{staly}.

\ird{Staly} is the same Neg\textsuperscript{0} head here as in plain equative
negation. The contrastive/focus force is a semantic consequence of negating an
identificational (exhaustive) predication, not a distinct grammatical function
of \ird{staly}. See §\,\ref{sec:clausal-negation} for the unified structural
account, §\,\ref{sec:vm-neg-exponents} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs} for the formal
DM analysis, and §\,\ref{sec:vm-ident-neg} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs} for the
full scope-theoretic analysis including the formal semantics of identificational
negation and the contrast table with propositional \ird{zá-}.

\subsection{The overt copular verb \ird{neh-}: transition and inchoative
predication}\label{sec:neh}

\subsubsection{Status and structural contrast with the zero copula}

The root \ird{neh-} is the overt copular verb of Iridian. Its most basic
function is to encode \term{transition}: the topic \synhead{DP} is asserted to
be moving from one classification or condition into another, or to have crossed
a threshold between them. This transitional meaning is not a pragmatic
implication but a semantic contribution of the root itself.

The structural difference between the zero copula and \ird{neh-} is not merely
that one is overt and the other silent. The zero copula projects \synhead{EquP},
a non-eventive predication layer with no \synhead{AspP}. \ird{Neh-}, by
contrast, projects a proper verbal predicate: it introduces a \term{transition
event} --- an eventuality whose onset, duration, and completion can be
aspectually individuated. This eventuality licenses Asp\textsuperscript{0},
which selects the \synhead{VP}, and the full verbal spine (\synhead{VP}
$\subset$ \synhead{AspP} $\subset$ \synhead{NegP}) is projected in the usual
way. The two structures contrast as follows:

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.42\textwidth}
\centering
\textit{Zero copula:}\\[6pt]
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{EquP}
    [{\synhead{DP}\\{\footnotesize topic}}]
    [{Eq$'$}
      [{\synhead{DP}\\{\footnotesize pred.}}]
      [{Eq$^{0}$\\{$\varnothing$}}]
    ]
  ]
\end{forest}
\end{minipage}%
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.52\textwidth}
\centering
\textit{Overt copula:}\\[6pt]
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{AspP}
    [{\synhead{DP}\\{\footnotesize topic}}]
    [{Asp$'$}
      [{Asp$^{0}$}]
      [\synhead{VP}
        [{\synhead{DP}\\{\footnotesize pred.}}]
        [{\ird{neh-}}]
      ]
    ]
  ]
\end{forest}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

\noindent The consequence for negation is immediate: since \ird{neh-} is a
verbal root embedded under \synhead{AspP}, the standard verbal negation strategy
applies. Neg\textsuperscript{0} is spelled out as the prefix \ird{zá-}, which
procliticizes to the left edge of the inflected form. \ird{Staly} does not arise
because the condition for its appearance --- the absence of a verbal host --- is
not satisfied.

\subsubsection{Paradigm}

The paradigm of \ird{neh-} is strongly suppletive and morphologically irregular;
principal forms are given in Table~\ref{tab:neh-paradigm}.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{Principal forms of \ird{neh-} (overt copula).}\label{tab:neh-paradigm}
  \medskip
  \begin{tabularx}{0.84\textwidth}{L l l}
    \toprule
    {\sc form} & {\sc affirmative} & {\sc negative} \\
    \midrule
    Continuous          & \ird{neha}   & \ird{záneha} \\
    Progressive         & \ird{nejme}  & \ird{zánejme} \\

\noindent The negative forms are entirely regular: \ird{zá-} (reduced to
\ird{za-} before unstressed syllables) procliticizes onto the corresponding
affirmative form. No suppletive negative root exists for \ird{neh-}; the
formation parallels that of all other finite verbs.

\subsubsection{Aspectual semantics}

\ird{Neh-} introduces a transition event, and its aspectual forms characterise
different perspectives on that event in the usual way.

The \textsc{continuous} (\ird{neha}) presents the transition as ongoing and
unbounded: the subject is in the process of moving into the new classification.
The inchoative flavour is inherent to \ird{neh-} itself, not to the continuous
aspect.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek doktor neha.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} doctor.\Dir{} \Cop{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek is becoming a doctor.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek doktor záneha.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} doctor.\Dir{} \Neg{}-\Cop{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek is not becoming a doctor.}//
\endgl
\xe

The \textsc{progressive} (\ird{nejme}) adds an agentive or effortful dimension:
the subject is actively engaged in the process of transition. Where the
continuous reports a change in progress, the progressive foregrounds the subject's
directed activity toward the new state.

\ex
\begingl
\gla Marek doktor nejme.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} doctor.\Dir{} \Cop{}-\Prog{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek is working toward becoming a doctor.}//
\endgl
\xe

The \Ctpl{} (\ird{nách}) locates the transition in prospective
time: the subject will enter the new classification. It is neutral between
imminent and more remote futures.

\ex
\begingl
\gla Marek doktor nách.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} doctor.\Dir{} \Cop{}-\Ctpl{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek will become a doctor.}//
\endgl
\xe

The \textsc{perfective} (\ird{nek}) presents the transition as a completed
event: the subject has crossed the threshold and entered the new state. The
resulting state is typically understood as still holding unless contextually
cancelled.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Tak byl kapitan nek.//
\glb \Dem{}.\Prox{} child.\Dir{} captain.\Dir{} \Cop{}-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{This child became captain.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Tak byl kapitan zánek.//
\glb \Dem{}.\Prox{} child.\Dir{} captain.\Dir{} \Neg{}-\Cop{}-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{This child did not become captain.}//
\endgl
\xe

\subsubsection{The retrospective and past equative predication}%
\label{sec:neh-past}

The \textsc{retrospective} (\ird{nac}) presents the transition as situated in a
past frame and viewed from the current moment. Its semantics are aspectually
neutral between the transitional reading (the subject entered the state at some
point in the past) and the stative reading (the subject was in the state during
the relevant past period).

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek doktor nac.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} doctor.\Dir{} \Cop{}-\Ret{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek was a doctor. / Marek had been a doctor.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek doktor zánac.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} doctor.\Dir{} \Neg{}-\Cop{}-\Ret{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek was not a doctor.}//
\endgl
\xe

This dual function of the retrospective is a direct consequence of the temporal
restriction on the zero copula. Since the zero copula is confined to
present-time and atemporal predications, past equative contexts require an overt
verb, and \ird{nac} is the form that fills this role. The transitional
implication that \ird{neh-} normally carries is therefore weakened or absent in
retrospective equatives: \ird{Marek doktor nac} does not necessarily imply that
Marek underwent a process of becoming a doctor, only that the doctor
classification held in the past. The implication is a pragmatic default, not a
semantic entailment of the retrospective form, and is readily cancelled by
context.

Whether the past classification still holds is not encoded. \ird{Marek doktor
nac} is compatible both with Marek's remaining a doctor and with his having
ceased to be one; the current-state inference, if present, is a conversational
implicature from the choice of a past-framed form over the zero-copula present.

The sequential converb \ird{nesu} --- colloquially \ird{nu} --- is a suppletive
form established independently of the regular aspect paradigm. Its
grammaticalisation as a discourse particle is described in
Section~\ref{sec:seq-copula} of Chapter~\ref{ch:complex}.

\section{Existential constructions}
\label{sec:existential-constructions}

\subsection{Overview}
\label{sec:existential-overview}%
\label{sec:jec}% retained for cross-reference compatibility
\label{sec:zad}% retained for cross-reference compatibility
\label{sec:jec-zad}% retained for cross-reference compatibility

Iridian expresses existential predication through a pair of suppletive verbal
roots that together constitute the affirmative/negative alternation of a single
functional head, Exist\textsuperscript{0}. The affirmative root \ird{jec-}
asserts the existence or presence of a referent; the negative root \ird{zad-}
denies it. Both inflect for the full aspect system; both are morphologically
irregular and suppletive, with the perfective and retrospective built on
distinct suppletive stems. The two paradigms are given together in
Table~\ref{tab:jec-zad-paradigm}.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{Principal forms of \ird{jec-} (\textsc{exst}) and \ird{zad-} (\textsc{neg.exst}).}\label{tab:jec-zad-paradigm}
  \medskip
  \begin{tabularx}{0.88\textwidth}{L l l}
    \toprule
    {\sc form} & \ird{jec-} (\textsc{exst}) & \ird{zad-} (\textsc{neg.exst}) \\
    \midrule
    Continuous          & \ird{jeca}   & \ird{zada} \\
    Progressive         & \ird{jecime} & \ird{zdíme} \\
    Contemplative       & \ird{jecach} & \ird{zadach} \\
    Perfective          & \ird{jak}    & \ird{zděk} \\
    Retrospective       & \ird{ján}   & \ird{znení} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

The two roots are not independent lexical items but are best understood as
suppletive allomorphs of the same Exist\textsuperscript{0} head: \ird{jec-} is
the default exponent, inserted when \synhead{NegP} is absent from the
derivation; \ird{zad-} replaces it when \synhead{NegP} is present, absorbing the
negative feature entirely and leaving Neg\textsuperscript{0} with no independent
PF reflex. The structural and distributional consequences of this analysis are
developed in §\,\ref{sec:neg-exst-alternation} and §\,\ref{sec:vm-jec-zad}
below. The division of labour between \ird{jec-} and the zero-copula
construction is loosely parallel to the Japanese distinction between the
existential verbs \textit{iru} and \textit{aru} on the one hand and the equative
\textit{da}/\textit{desu} on the other: \ird{jec-} covers existence and
presence; zero copula covers identity and class membership.

Iridian has three main productive uses of the existential construction: to
assert existence or presence, including location in a particular place
(\S\,\ref{sec:existence-and-presence}), to express possession
(\S\,\ref{sec:existence-possession}), and certain idiomatic presentational
constructions (\S\,\ref{sec:existential-presentatives}).


\subsection{Existence and presence}\label{sec:existence-and-presence}

The primary use of \ird{jec-} is to assert that a referent exists or is present
at the moment of utterance or within a contextually given frame. The entity in
question stands in the direct case. \ird{Zad-} forms the direct negative
counterpart, asserting non-existence or absence under the same structural
conditions.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Byl jeca.//
\glb child.\Dir{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is a child. / A child is present.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Byl zada.//
\glb child.\Dir{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is no child. / No child is present.}//
\endgl
\xe

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Kroumaště na pivo s jeca.//
\glb refrigerator.\Ind{} in beer.\Dir{} still \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is still (some) beer left in the refrigerator.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Kroumaště na pivo zada.//
\glb refrigerator.\Ind{} in beer.\Dir{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is no beer in the refrigerator.}//
\endgl
\xe

Within the Minimalist framework, \ird{jec-} is the phonological exponent of a
dedicated functional head, \term{Exist}\textsuperscript{0}, which projects
\term{ExistP} as the innermost stratum of the clause spine --- the slot occupied
by root \synhead{VP} in ordinary verbal clauses. The sole argument it licenses
is the \term{existential pivot}: the \synhead{DP} asserted to exist, bearing the
direct case as the complement of Exist\textsuperscript{0}. ExistP is then
selected by Asp\textsuperscript{0}, which contributes the aspect suffix, and
feeds the higher functional sequence including \synhead{NegP}. When
\synhead{NegP} is absent, Exist\textsuperscript{0} is spelled out as \ird{jec-};
when \synhead{NegP} is present, suppletive Vocabulary Insertion replaces
\ird{jec-} with \ird{zad-}, leaving Neg\textsuperscript{0} with no independent
PF exponent. The affirmative and negative structures of \ird{Byl jeca} and
\ird{Byl zada} are shown below:

\begin{center}
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{AspP}

\noindent The pivot surfaces to the left as the absolutive topic constituent,
base-generated preverbal under head-finality; no movement is required. In the
affirmative it occupies Spec,\synhead{AspP}; in the negative it occupies Spec,\synhead{NegP}. The
Neg\textsuperscript{0} feature is discharged by conditioning the suppletive
readjustment \ird{jec-} $\rightarrow$ \ird{zad-} at Exist\textsuperscript{0},
and has no residual PF exponent of its own.

\paragraph{Location.} \ird{Jec-} is also used to locate an entity: the entity
appears in the direct case and the location is expressed by an indirect noun
phrase followed by a postposed clitic adposition. \ird{Zad-} negates location,
asserting that the referent is not present at the named location.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek dumě na jeca.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} house.\Ind{} in \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek is at home.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Marek dumě na zada.//
\glb Marek.\Dir{} house.\Ind{} in \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Marek is not at home. / Marek is absent.}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent The locative phrase is merged as a PP adjunct to ExistP, introducing
the spatial domain within which the existential relation holds, without altering
the valency of Exist\textsuperscript{0}. The pivot remains the sole direct
argument; the structures parallel those for bare existentials above, with the PP
adjoined at the ExistP periphery in both affirmative and negative derivations.


\subsection{Possession}\label{sec:existence-possession}

Iridian has no separate verb meaning \trsl{have}. What English presents as
\trsl{I have X} is in Iridian construed as \trsl{there is X at me}: the
possessor stands in the indirect case, the possessed thing in the direct, and
the construction is a special case of existential predication. \ird{Zad-}
produces the negative possessive, asserting that the possessed item does not
exist within the possessor's sphere.

The ordinary arrangement of the clause places the possessor phrase first, the
possessed noun next, and the existential verb last:

\begin{center}
[\emph{possessor}.\Ind{} \ \ird{na}] \ \ [\emph{possessee}.\Dir{}] \ \ \ird{jec-} / \ird{zad-}
\end{center}

\noindent The possessed noun is the thing whose existence is asserted or denied
relative to the possessor as reference point. In grammatical terms it is the
existential pivot --- the direct argument of Exist\textsuperscript{0} --- while
the possessor is an oblique locative domain, a PP adjunct to ExistP, within
which the existential relation holds.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Bylam na tóm jeca.//
\glb child.\Ind{} in/on book.\Dir{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{The child has a book.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Bylam na tóm zada.//
\glb child.\Ind{} in/on book.\Dir{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{The child does not have a book.}//
\endgl
\xe

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Tóm bylam na jeca.//
\glb book.\Dir{} child.\Ind{} in/on \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{As for the book, it is with the child.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Tóm bylam na zada.//
\glb book.\Dir{} child.\Ind{} in/on \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{As for the book, the child does not have it.}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent The second pair illustrates that topic-initial order is fully
available with \ird{zad-} on the same terms as with \ird{jec-}: the alternation
belongs to the preverbal field and is independent of the affirmative/negative
opposition.

Following Freeze (1992) and the localist tradition, possession is treated here
as a structural subtype of location: \trsl{the book exists at the child}. The
possessor phrase [\textit{possessor}.\Ind{} \ird{na}] and a plain locative
phrase [\textit{location}.\Ind{} \ird{na}] are structurally parallel: both are
oblique PPs that modify ExistP without altering the valency of
Exist\textsuperscript{0}. The affirmative and negative possessive structures
are:

\begin{center}
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [ExistP
    [{PP\\\ird{bylam na}\\{\footnotesize possessor}}]
    [ExistP
      [{\synhead{DP}\\\textit{tóm}\\{\footnotesize book.\Dir{}}\\{\scriptsize pivot}}]
      [{Exist$'$}
        [{Exist\\\ird{jec-}}]
      ]
    ]
  ]
\end{forest}
\hspace{2em}
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{NegP}
    [{\synhead{DP}\\\textit{tóm}\\{\footnotesize book.\Dir{}}\\{\scriptsize pivot}}]
    [{Neg$'$}
      [{Neg\\{\small($\varnothing$)}}]
      [\synhead{AspP}
        [{Asp$'$}
          [{Asp\\\ird{-a}\\{\footnotesize\textsc{[cont]}}}]
          [ExistP
            [{PP\\\ird{bylam na}\\{\footnotesize possessor}}]
            [ExistP
              [{Exist$'$}
                [{Exist\\\ird{zad-}\\{\scriptsize suppletive}}]
              ]
            ]
          ]
        ]
      ]
    ]
  ]
\end{forest}
\end{center}

\noindent The structural parallelism between locative and possessive
existentials is a direct consequence of this unified analysis: both
constructions merge a PP at the ExistP periphery, and the only grammatical
difference is the semantic content of the oblique phrase itself. The suppletive
readjustment applies identically in both frames; the PP adjunct is unaffected by
the presence or absence of \synhead{NegP}.

Since possession is expressed through the existential verb, possessive clauses
naturally inflect across the full aspectual system, and \ird{jec-} and
\ird{zad-} participate symmetrically in these aspectual contrasts. The
continuous is the common unmarked form: \ird{Bylam na tóm jeca} \trsl{the child
has a book}; \ird{Bylam na tóm zada} \trsl{the child does not have a book}. The
progressive conveys a relation in the course of arising: \ird{Bylam na tóm
jecime} \trsl{the child is getting a book}. The contemplative projects into
prospect: \ird{Bylam na tóm jecach} \trsl{the child will have a book}. The
perfective, applied to so stative a predicate, yields an inchoative sense:
\ird{Bylam na tóm jak} \trsl{the child got a book}. The retrospective places the
relation in a prior frame: \ird{Bylam na tóm žac} \trsl{the child had a book}.
In all these forms the construction remains fundamentally existential: the
possessed noun remains the direct pivot, the possessor remains oblique, and the
aspect alters only the speaker's view of the relation.

Since the possessed noun is the existential pivot, indefinite possessees are
especially natural. Definite possessees are not excluded, but with them the
clause more readily shades toward a meaning of location, availability, or
custody rather than settled ownership --- a consequence of the existential
source of the construction.


\subsection{Event-anchored existential pivots}\label{sec:existential-presentatives}

The existential predicate in Iridian consistently selects a nominal pivot: the
entity asserted to exist stands as the direct-case complement of
Exist\textsuperscript{0}. The constructional family defined by this selectional
requirement is broader than the overt-head presentational use treated in earlier
descriptions. Three subtypes can be distinguished according to the internal
structure of the pivot \synhead{DP}:

\begin{enumerate}[wide]
\item a bare \synhead{NP} (plain existential; §\,\ref{sec:existence-and-presence}),
\item a complex \synhead{DP} with an overt lexical head modified by a
  participant-oriented attributive (overt-head presentational;
  §\,\ref{sec:exst-overt-head}),
\item a headless \synhead{DP} whose sole content is a participant nominal
  derived from verbal structure by Iridian's participant-nominal morphology
  (headless participant-nominal; §\,\ref{sec:exst-headless-nmlz}).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The three subtypes share a single structural identity: all three
occupy Spec,\synhead{ExistP} as the nominal complement of
Exist\textsuperscript{0}. They differ not in the matrix construction but in the
internal architecture of the pivot \synhead{DP}.

\subsubsection{Architectural decision: participant nominals, not silent nouns}
\label{sec:exst-pivot-arch}

The third subtype requires an explicit analytical decision. When the existential
pivot introduces an otherwise unidentified participant --- the function served
by English \trsl{somebody}, \trsl{something}, \trsl{nobody}, \trsl{nothing} ---
a possible analysis would posit an indefinite null lexical noun ($\varnothing$N)
heading the pivot, with the verbal description functioning as its attributive
modifier. That analysis is explicitly rejected for Iridian.

Iridian does not ordinarily lexicalize English-style \trsl{someone},
\trsl{something}, \trsl{no one}, and \trsl{nothing} as free pronominal items in
clause structure, and it does not build their equivalents by projecting a
semantically bleached null noun. Instead, the indefinite pivot is a bona fide
participant nominal: an nP/DP derived directly from verbal structure by the
language's participant-nominal morphology (§\,\ref{sec:nmlz-predicative} of
Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs}). The nominal character of the pivot is the output of
n\textsuperscript{0}, whose categorial feature nominalises the embedded verbal
structure. No lexical noun is inserted or covertly present; the nominalization
is the whole pivot.

This treatment is principled within the grammar's DM architecture. Iridian already has a
robust system of participant nominals whose orientation is determined by voice morphology
(§\,\ref{sec:nmlz-predicative} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs}): antipassive stems yield
agent-oriented nominals, unmarked transitive stems yield patient-oriented ones,
and
applicative stems yield nominals for the promoted participant. The indefinite existential
construction draws on precisely this system. The participant orientation of the
existential pivot is encoded by the same voice-sensitive stem morphology that governs
participant nominals throughout the grammar; no independent mechanism is required.

\subsubsection{Overt-head presentational pivots}
\label{sec:exst-overt-head}

The overt-head presentational construction is the most structurally elaborated
subtype. The pivot \synhead{DP} consists of a lexical head noun in the direct
case modified by a participant-oriented attributive. Its canonical shape is:

\begin{center}
[\textit{internal-arg.\Ind{}} \ \textit{participant-oriented-attributive} \ \textit{head-NP}.\Dir{}] \ \ \ird{jeca}
\end{center}

The modifier is canonically oriented toward the participant the embedded stem
privileges: the patient or theme of a transitive predicate, the recipient or
beneficiary of an applicative stem, or more generally the non-agentive
participant that emerges from the event as the discourse-relevant referent. The
construction therefore introduces \trsl{a letter that the woman wrote} or
\trsl{a child someone fed} rather than asserting the writing or the feeding for
its own sake.

\ird{Jeca} is fixed in this use. Temporal and aspectual interpretation come
entirely from the embedded attributive, which may be perfective, retrospective,
continuous, progressive, or contemplative. The matrix \ird{jeca} signals only
that the referent is being introduced into the current discourse.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Obrohnikový senica jeca.//
\glb read-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} news.\Dir{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is some news that (I) read.} \lingcontext{patient/theme introduced}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Že dbornikový kíl jeca.//
\glb \textsc{1sg}.\Ind{} hear-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} song.\Dir{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is a song that I heard.} \lingcontext{stimulus introduced via perception event}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Vednikový ryc jeca.//
\glb see-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} cat.\Dir{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is a cat that (I) saw.} \lingcontext{perceived referent introduced via completed event}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Verce blětébový byl jeca.//
\glb toy.\Ind{} give-\Appl{}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} child.\Dir{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is a child that he gave a toy to.} \lingcontext{recipient introduced via transfer event}//
\endgl
\xe

The attributive is an adjunct within the pivot \synhead{DP}; it is not a
predicate of the matrix clause and contributes no independent propositional
content at the matrix level. The matrix Exist\textsuperscript{0} asserts only
$\textsc{exist}(x)$ for the complex, event-anchored pivot $x$. This
decomposition directly accounts for the fixedness of \ird{jeca}: because the
existential head's semantic contribution is reduced to $\textsc{exist}(x)$, the
least-marked aspect (continuous, unbounded) is the appropriate exponent; any
other aspect would impose a viewpoint on the existential state itself,
conflicting with the temporal interpretation already contributed by the embedded
attributive.

\begin{center}
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{AspP}
    [{\synhead{DP}\\{\footnotesize pivot}}

      [{\synhead{NP}\\\ird{senica}\\{\footnotesize news.\Dir{}}}]
    ]
    [{Asp$'$}
      [{Asp\\\ird{jec-a}\\{\scriptsize Exist+Cont}}]

          [{Exist\\\ird{jec-}}]

      ]
    ]
  ]
\end{forest}
\end{center}

Because the function of this construction is to introduce a specific new
referent into the discourse model, non-agentive pivot heads are strongly
favoured. Agents are the prototypical topical and volitional participants; they
are characteristically presupposed or previously introduced, making them poor
existential pivots. Patients, themes, recipients, and other non-agentive
participants are characteristically discourse-new and are therefore the natural
heads of a presentational pivot \synhead{DP}.

\ex
\begingl
\gla ?Senicěte obroždkový chrás jeca.//
\glb news.\Ind{}.\Def{} read-\textsc{antip}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} person.\Dir{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is a person who read the news.} \lingcontext{formally possible, but
  marked: agentive head disfavoured in overt-head presentational}//
\endgl
\xe

This discourse preference is, however, a property of the overt-head subtype
specifically. The headless participant-nominal construction
(§\,\ref{sec:exst-headless-nmlz}) is the default indefinite strategy across all
participant roles and does not inherit any preference for non-agentive pivots.

The negative counterpart of the overt-head presentational is formed with
\ird{zada}. \synhead{NegP} scopes over ExistP, and the construction asserts that
no referent satisfying the event-anchored description exists. As with
\ird{jeca}, the continuous form \ird{zada} is fixed; the aspectual
interpretation of the embedded event comes entirely from the attributive.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Obrohnikový senica zada.//
\glb read-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} news.\Dir{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is no news that (I) read.} \lingcontext{negative overt-head presentational; event-anchored referent denied}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Verce blětébový byl zada.//
\glb toy.\Ind{} give-\Appl{}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} child.\Dir{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is no child that he gave a toy to.} \lingcontext{negative presentational; recipient referent denied}//
\endgl
\xe

\begin{center}
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{NegP}
    [{\synhead{DP}\\{\footnotesize pivot}}
      [{AttrP\\\ird{obrohnikový}\\{\footnotesize read-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr}}}]
      [{\synhead{NP}\\\ird{senica}\\{\footnotesize news.\Dir{}}}]
    ]

      [{Neg\\{\small($\varnothing$)}}]
      [\synhead{AspP}
        [{Asp$'$}

            [{Exist$'$}

\end{forest}
\end{center}

\noindent Exist\textsuperscript{0} now asserts $\neg\textsc{exist}(x)$ for the
complex pivot: no referent satisfying the event-anchored description exists. The
attributive remains an adjunct within the pivot \synhead{DP} with no
matrix-level propositional force. The disfavour of agentive pivot heads carries
over to \ird{zada} for the same reasons as in the affirmative.

The \ird{zad-} existential takes a singular pivot; plural participant nominals
are not licensed in the negative existential frame.

\subsubsection{Headless participant-nominal pivots}
\label{sec:exst-headless-nmlz}

The headless participant-nominal construction employs a pivot \synhead{DP} that
contains no lexical head noun. The pivot's entire content is a participant
nominal derived from verbal structure, whose voice morphology determines
participant orientation. This construction is the default means of expressing
indefinite participant reference in Iridian.

The participant nominal is built by the same morphological process that produces
the identificational predicates of §\,\ref{sec:nmlz-predicative} of
Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs}: n\textsuperscript{0} (spelled out as \ird{-ou}, with
regular stem-final contractions) applies to a verbal structure in which voice
and aspect have already been specified. The participant orientation follows from
the voice of the embedded stem:

\begin{itemize}
  \item an antipassive-stem nominal denotes the agent of the event (\trsl{the
  one who Verb'd});
  \item an unmarked transitive-stem nominal denotes the patient or theme
  (\trsl{that which was Verb'd});
  \item an applicative-stem nominal denotes the promoted participant (\trsl{the
  one who was Verb'd-to}).
\end{itemize}

\noindent When such a nominal occupies the existential pivot position,
Exist\textsuperscript{0} asserts the existence of an individual satisfying the
participant description. The event description is internal to the nominalized
pivot, not a contribution of the matrix predicate. The matrix \ird{jec-}
contributes only $\textsc{exist}(x)$; the verbal material, together with its
aspect and voice, supplies the property that identifies the individual
introduced.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Ščenikou jeca.//
\glb arrive-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is someone who arrived.} \lingcontext{agentive indefinite: intransitive nominal, agent orientation}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Lubcí piaščikou jeca.//
\glb apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is someone who ate an apple.} \lingcontext{agentive indefinite: antipassive agent-nominal}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Vednikou jeca.//
\glb see-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is something (I) saw.} \lingcontext{patient indefinite: transitive patient-nominal}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Verce blětébovnikou jeca.//
\glb toy.\Ind{} give-\Appl{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is someone who was given a toy.} \lingcontext{goal indefinite: applicative recipient-nominal}//
\endgl
\xe

The formal structure of the headless participant-nominal existential differs
from the overt-head construction only in the internal architecture of the pivot.
The nP directly daughter-of-DP contains the Voice/Asp/Root complex;
n\textsuperscript{0} introduces nominal category; no lexical NP head is present.

\begin{center}
\begin{forest}
  syntax tree
  [\synhead{AspP}
    [{\synhead{DP}\\{\footnotesize pivot}}
      [{\synhead{nP}}
        [{\synhead{VoiceP}\\{Root + Voice/Asp}}]
        [{n\textsuperscript{0}\\\ird{-ou}\\{\scriptsize\Nmlz{}}}]
      ]
    ]
    [{Asp$'$}
      [{Asp\\\ird{jec-a}\\{\scriptsize Exist+Cont}}]
      [ExistP
        [{Exist$'$}
          [{Exist\\\ird{jec-}}]
        ]
      ]
    ]
  ]
\end{forest}
\end{center}

\noindent The semantics is parallel to the plain and overt-head cases. The nP
denotes a property of individuals, event-anchored by the verbal material it
encodes; Exist\textsuperscript{0} introduces a witness individual satisfying
that property. The eventive content is entirely internal to the pivot; no
clausal embedding of the verbal structure into the matrix occurs.

The contrast between the two constructional subtypes is already visible in the
grammar's existential question--answer paradigm:

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Vednikou by jeca?//
\glb see-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \textsc{q} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Did you see anything?} \lingcontext{headless NMLZ pivot; referent as yet unidentified}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Vednikový ryc jeca.//
\glb see-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} cat.\Dir{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is a cat that I saw.} \lingcontext{overt-head pivot; specific referent introduced}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent In (a), the pivot is a headless nominal (\ird{vednikou}), because no
referent has yet been identified: the question asks whether any individual
satisfying the existential description exists. In (b), the pivot is a complex
\synhead{DP} with an overt lexical head, because the cat is now a specific
entity being introduced into the discourse model. These are not competing
strategies but complementary options: the headless construction is the
appropriate form when a participant is entirely unidentified; the overt-head
construction is the appropriate form when a referent is specific enough to
anchor to a lexical category and an event description.

\subsubsection{Indefinite participant introduction}
\label{sec:exst-indef-pos}

The headless participant-nominal construction is the primary means by which
Iridian expresses what English achieves with the free indefinite pronouns
\trsl{somebody}, \trsl{someone}, \trsl{something}, and \trsl{anything}. Free
pronominal items are not the ordinary Iridian strategy; the existential pivot is
instead a participant nominal whose voice morphology encodes the relevant
participant role.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Ščenikou jeca.//
\glb arrive-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Someone arrived.} \lingcontext{lit.\ there is one-who-arrived; agentive indefinite}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Lubcí piaščikou jeca.//
\glb apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Someone ate the apple.} \lingcontext{lit.\ there is one-who-ate-it; agentive transitive indefinite}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Vednikou jeca.//
\glb see-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{(I) saw something.} \lingcontext{lit.\ there is a seen-thing; patient indefinite}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Verce blětébovnikou jeca.//
\glb toy.\Ind{} give-\Appl{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Someone was given a toy.} \lingcontext{lit.\ there is one-who-was-given-a-toy; goal indefinite}//
\endgl
\xe

Agentive indefinites in (a) and (b) are fully natural. This is a significant
difference from the overt-head subtype, where agent-oriented pivot heads are
disfavoured. The discourse preference for non-agentive pivots is a property of
the overt-head presentational construction specifically, grounded in its
referent-introduction function. The headless construction asserts only that some
participant of the relevant type exists; because this function is indifferent to
the discourse-topic properties that make agents poor existential pivots in the
overt-head type, agentive participant nominals are unproblematic here.

Older descriptive treatments noted that Iridian systematically reframes English
indefinite-pronoun clauses as existential constructions built from
nominalization, rather than employing dedicated indefinite pronominal items. The
present analysis gives that observation formal content: the indefinite
existential is not a construction containing a silent lexical noun but one in
which the participant nominal itself is the entire pivot, with participant
orientation encoded by voice morphology internal to n\textsuperscript{0}'s
complement.

\subsubsection{Negative existential indefinites}
\label{sec:exst-indef-neg}

Negative indefinites --- English \trsl{nobody}, \trsl{no one}, \trsl{nothing}
--- are expressed by the negative existential \ird{zad-} with a
participant-nominal pivot. The derivation is structurally parallel to the
positive: \synhead{NegP} is projected, Neg\textsuperscript{0} triggers
suppletive Vocabulary Insertion at Exist\textsuperscript{0}, and the resulting
\ird{zad-} asserts $\neg\textsc{exist}(x)$ for the individual satisfying the
nominal description.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Ščenikou zada.//
\glb arrive-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Nobody arrived.} \lingcontext{lit.\ there is no one-who-arrived}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Lubcí piaščikou zada.//
\glb apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Nobody ate the apple.} \lingcontext{lit.\ there is no one-who-ate-it}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Vednikou zada.//
\glb see-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Nothing was seen. / (I) saw nothing.} \lingcontext{lit.\ there is no seen-thing}//
\endgl
\xe

The negation is existential: no individual satisfying the participant
description exists. No presupposition is generated about whether the relevant
event occurred. This property distinguishes negative existential indefinites
from identificational negation, treated in the following subsection.

\subsubsection{Relation to identificational equatives}
\label{sec:exst-vs-ident}

The same participant nominals that serve as existential pivots with
\ird{jec-}/\ird{zad-} also serve as predicates in identificational equatives
(§\,\ref{sec:ident-equative}). The two constructions must be carefully
distinguished, because they differ both in structural position and in semantic
scope.

In an identificational equative, the participant nominal is the predicative
complement of a zero-copula clause. The topic \synhead{DP} is identified as the
unique entity satisfying the description; the event is presupposed. Negation of
this construction with \ird{staly} yields identificational negation: the
presupposed event remains intact, and only the identity of the relevant
participant is denied.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek lubcí piaščikou.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is the one who ate the apple.} \lingcontext{identificational equative: event presupposed; Janek identified as agent}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek lubcí piaščikou staly.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \NegCop{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is not the one who ate the apple.} \lingcontext{identificational negation: event still presupposed; Janek misidentified as agent}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent The contrastive implication that someone else ate the apple arises
because identificational predications are exhaustive: negating an exhaustive
identification entails the existence of a different satisfier. This is the
expected interpretation in discourse (§\,\ref{sec:ident-equative}).

The existential construction with a participant-nominal pivot has a
fundamentally different semantic profile:

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Lubcí piaščikou jeca.//
\glb apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Someone ate the apple.} \lingcontext{existential: asserts existence of an agent; no event presupposition}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Lubcí piaščikou zada.//
\glb apple.\Ind{} eat-\Antip{}-\Pf{}-\Nmlz{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Nobody ate the apple.} \lingcontext{negative existential: no agent exists; event not presupposed}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent In the existential, the participant nominal is an argument (the pivot)
in Spec,\synhead{ExistP}; \ird{zad-} scopes over Exist\textsuperscript{0},
denying the existence of any individual satisfying the description. No event
presupposition survives. The contrast in structural position --- nominal as
predicate vs.\ nominal as argument --- drives the contrast in presuppositional
profile.

Ordinary negative indefinites (\trsl{nobody}, \trsl{nothing}) therefore belong
to the existential system with \ird{zad-}, not to the identificational equative
with \ird{staly}. The identificational construction is the appropriate choice
only when the discourse specifically presupposes the event and contests the
identity of its participant. Deploying \ird{staly} + participant nominal in a
context where no such presupposition is intended would produce an unintended
exhaustivity implication that the event occurred and some other participant was
responsible.

A small set of items --- including \ird{niho} and \ird{zide} --- appear to
function as negative-polarity or quantificational operators in certain registers
and may interact with both the existential and identificational systems. Their
full characterization remains an open question and is not pursued here.

\subsubsection{Discourse profile of the two constructional subtypes}
\label{sec:exst-discourse}

The overt-head and headless participant-nominal constructions have complementary
discourse profiles. They are not competing strategies for the same communicative
function but are specialized for different referential tasks.

The overt-head presentational construction is the natural means of introducing a
specific, contentfully described new referent into the discourse model. The
lexical head noun provides a category for the referent; the attributive anchors
it to an event in which it is the salient participant. This construction is
especially apt when the speaker wishes to present an entity rich enough in
description to support further reference --- to introduce a cat, a song, a piece
of news, or a child given a toy. Because referent introduction presupposes that
the referent is hearer-new and non-presupposed, non-agentive participants are
the most natural pivot heads; their discourse-new status is the norm, while
agents are characteristically already in play.

The headless participant-nominal construction performs a different function: it
asserts the existence of an otherwise unidentified participant without assigning
that participant a lexical category. It is the appropriate form when the speaker
wishes only to assert that some individual, as yet unnamed and uncharacterized,
bears the relevant participant relation to an event. This construction is
indifferent to participant role: agentive, patient, and goal pivots are all
equally regular.

The presentational construction is not simply a periphrastic variant of the
corresponding eventive clause. \ird{Vednikový ryc jeca} and the corresponding
plain verbal clause share the same truth conditions --- both are true when the
speaker saw a cat --- but they differ in discourse function. The plain clause
reports an event of seeing; the overt-head presentational performs a distinct
discourse act: the introduction of the cat as a new participant into the
discourse model, with the seeing event supplying the identifying anchor. The
distinction is sharpest in context. After the existential question \ird{Vednikou
by jeca?} (\trsl{Did you see anything?}), the overt-head answer \ird{Vednikový
ryc jeca} is highly felicitous because the question has already made the entity
--- not the event --- the relevant discourse target. After a question targeting
the agent (\trsl{Who saw the cat?}), the overt-head presentational is a poor
response: the construction is designed to bring a non-agentive participant onto
the discourse stage, and the question has made the agent the target instead. The
headless construction, by contrast, is the default form for asserting the
existence of any participant type, agentive or otherwise, when no richer
description is yet warranted.


\subsection{Suppletive exclusivity: \ird{jec-}, \ird{zad-}, and the \synhead{NegP} alternation}\label{sec:neg-exst-alternation}%
\label{sec:vm-jec-zad}%
\label{sec:vm-jec}

The account in this section is the Exist\textsuperscript{0}-local unpacking of
the Neg\textsuperscript{0} allomorphy analysis in §\,\ref{sec:vm-neg-exponents}
of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs}. That analysis characterises \ird{zad-} as the
existential exponent of Neg\textsuperscript{0}: \synhead{NegP} is projected, and
its Neg\textsuperscript{0} feature is discharged by conditioning suppletive
Vocabulary Insertion at Exist\textsuperscript{0}, leaving Neg\textsuperscript{0}
with no independent PF reflex. The present section redescribes the same facts
from the perspective of Exist\textsuperscript{0}: \ird{jec-} and \ird{zad-} are
suppletive allomorphs of the same functional head, with \synhead{NegP} presence
as the conditioning environment. The two descriptions are notational variants of
one analysis, not competing accounts.

\ird{Jec-} and \ird{zad-} are in strict complementary distribution conditioned
by the presence or absence of \synhead{NegP} in the derivation. Wherever
\synhead{NegP} is absent, Exist\textsuperscript{0} is spelled out as \ird{jec-};
wherever \synhead{NegP} is present, it is spelled out as \ird{zad-} and
\ird{jec-} is excluded. This is an instance of the same conditioned allomorphy
that governs Neg\textsuperscript{0}'s exponence across all Iridian clause types:
\ird{zá-} (prefix) in verbal clauses, \ird{staly} (free morpheme) in equative
clauses, and suppletive root replacement in existential clauses. The three are
not separate lexical negation strategies but conditioned phonological reflexes
of a single \synhead{NegP} head.

The semantic content of the alternation is straightforwardly compositional.
\ird{Jec-} contributes $\textsc{exist}(x)$; \ird{zad-} contributes
$\neg\textsc{exist}(x)$, where the negation is the propositional negation
licensed by \synhead{NegP}. The same opposition is preserved across all
construction types --- bare existential, locative, possessive, and
presentational --- and there is no grammatical asymmetry between the two roots
in terms of which construction types each permits.

The sole constructional asymmetry is pragmatic. In the presentational use, both
\ird{jeca} and \ird{zada} are fixed continuous forms, but the positive
presentational functions as a discourse-introducing device (it asserts and
presents a new referent), whereas the negative presentational presupposes the
availability of the event-anchored description in order to deny a referent
matching it. This follows from general properties of existential negation and
requires no grammatical stipulation.

\paragraph{The impossibility of \ird{*zájec-}.} A natural question is whether
the NEG prefix \ird{zá-} can be used with \ird{jec-} to form a negative
existential. It cannot. Forms like \ird{*zájeca} and \ird{*zájecime} are
ungrammatical across all existential construction types; negative existential
predication is always and exclusively expressed by \ird{zad-}.

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Bylam na tóm zada.//
\glb child.\Ind{} in/on book.\Dir{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{The child does not have a book.} \lingcontext{\ird{zad-} obligatory in the negative possessive}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla {*}Bylam na tóm zájeca.//
\glb child.\Ind{} in/on book.\Dir{} \Neg{}-\Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{(intended: the child does not have a book)} \lingcontext{\ird{zá-} cannot prefix \ird{jec-}}//
\endgl
\xe

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Obrohnikový senica zada.//
\glb read-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} news.\Dir{} \NegExst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{There is no news that (I) read.} \lingcontext{\ird{zad-} obligatory in the negative presentational}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla {*}Obrohnikový senica zájeca.//
\glb read-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{attr} news.\Dir{} \Neg{}-\Exst{}-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{(intended: there is no news that (I) read)} \lingcontext{\ird{zá-} cannot prefix \ird{jec-}}//
\endgl
\xe

The structural basis is straightforward. When Neg\textsuperscript{0} is present
in an existential clause, it triggers suppletive Vocabulary Insertion at the
Exist\textsuperscript{0} node, replacing \ird{jec-} with \ird{zad-}. The
Neg\textsuperscript{0} feature is thereby discharged structurally in full; no
\synhead{NegP} head remains to be spelled out as \ird{zá-}. The prefix \ird{zá-}
is the PF reflex of Neg\textsuperscript{0} only when Neg\textsuperscript{0} has
not already been discharged through suppletive readjustment. In verbal clauses,
where no suppletive alternation is available at the predicate root, \ird{zá-} is
the sole exponent; in existential clauses, the suppletive root exhausts the
Neg\textsuperscript{0} feature and \ird{zá-} accordingly never surfaces.

The form \ird{*zájeca} would require Neg\textsuperscript{0} to be doubly
exponed: once as the suppletive trigger replacing \ird{jec-} at
Exist\textsuperscript{0}, and again as a prefix attaching to the surviving
positive root. Since a single syntactic head cannot discharge twice, this
configuration is structurally ill-formed. The same logic excludes \ird{*zázada}:
stacking \ird{zá-} onto the already-negative \ird{zad-} would require two
independent Neg\textsuperscript{0} heads in the same clause, violating the
uniqueness of the \synhead{NegP} projection. The prohibition on \ird{zá-} in
existential clauses and the prohibition on \ird{*zázad-} are therefore the same
prohibition, both following from the single-\synhead{NegP} constraint on the
clause spine.

\section{Negation in clausal context}\label{sec:clausal-negation}

At the clausal level, negation in Iridian is expressed by a single functional
projection, \synhead{NegP}, whose Neg\textsuperscript{0} head occupies position
4 in the clause spine (above \synhead{AspP}, below \synhead{IP}/\synhead{MoodP};
see Table~\ref{tab:th-clause-spine} in §\,\ref{sec:th-clause-structure}). The
surface form of Neg\textsuperscript{0} varies by predicate type, yielding three
distinct phonological reflexes that together cover all clause types. This
section provides a descriptive account of the distribution; the formal DM
analysis of Neg\textsuperscript{0} allomorphy (including the prefix paradox and
the unified \synhead{NegP} argument) is developed in §\,\ref{sec:vm-neg} and
§\,\ref{sec:vm-neg-exponents} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs};
Figure~\ref{fig:neg0-comparative} there provides the comparative structural
summary of the three exponents in a single diagram.

\subsection{Verbal clauses: \ird{zá-}}

In finite verbal clauses, Neg\textsuperscript{0} is spelled out as the prefix
\ird{zá-} (reduced to \ird{za-} in unstressed environments). Because the verbal
complex is clause-final under head-finality, and because \ird{zá-} has no
independent prosodic weight, it procliticizes to the left edge of the verbal
complex. The \synhead{NegP} head is syntactically above \synhead{AspP}, but PF
cliticization places the prefix before the root --- a phonological rather than a
syntactic inversion. See §\,\ref{sec:negation} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs} and
§\,\ref{sec:vm-neg} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs} for the full analysis.

\subsection{Equative clauses: \ird{staly}}

In zero-copula equative and class-membership clauses, no verbal complex is
present, and \ird{zá-} therefore has no host to cliticize onto.
Neg\textsuperscript{0} is instead spelled out as the free morpheme \ird{staly},
which surfaces in the canonical head-final position of a \synhead{NegP} head:
clause-finally, after the topic \synhead{DP} and the predicative complement
\synhead{DP}. This is the position the clause spine directly predicts.
\ird{Staly} is morphologically invariant; it bears no aspect, mood, or person
marking of any kind, consistent with its status as a functional head rather than
a verbal root. See §\,\ref{sec:zero-copula} for exemplification of predicational
equative negation (\ird{Marek doktor staly}) and §\,\ref{sec:ident-equative} for
identificational equative negation (\ird{Lobek Jancí piaštnikou staly}).

\subsection{Identificational negation: scope contrast with \ird{zá-}}

The availability of participant nominal predicates (§\,\ref{sec:ident-equative})
introduces a structurally motivated contrast between two types of clausal
negation that share the same morphological means but differ in semantic scope:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em, labelwidth=!, labelindent=0em]
\item[\upshape Propositional negation (\ird{zá-}).] The prefix \ird{zá-} negates
  the event description expressed by the finite verb. The negated clause denies
  that the event occurred (or was performed by the relevant participant). No
  presupposition is generated about whether the event occurred in some other
  form.
\item[\upshape Identificational negation (\ird{staly} + participant nominal).]
  The combination of a participant nominal predicate and \ird{staly} negates the
  identification: the topic \synhead{DP} is denied as the unique satisfier of
  the nominalised description. The event is presupposed to have occurred; only
  the identity of the participant is in question.
\end{description}

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Lobek Janku zápiaštnik.//
\glb apple.\Dir{} Janek.\Trs{} \Neg{}-eat-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek did not eat the apple.}
      \lingcontext{propositional: the eating may not have occurred at all}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Lobek Jancí piaštnikou staly.//
\glb apple.\Dir{} Janek.\Ind{} eat-\textsc{trs}-\Pf{}-\textsc{nmlz} \NegCop{}//
\glft \trsl{The apple is not what Janek ate.}
      \lingcontext{identificational: eating presupposed; the apple is the wrong identification}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent The contrastive implication (`it was something else') in (b) arises
because identificational predications are exhaustive: asserting that $x$ is the
entity satisfying the description entails that no other entity does. Negating an
exhaustive assertion therefore entails the existence of a different satisfier.
This implication is pragmatically cancellable in principle but is the strongly
expected interpretation in discourse. For the formal scope analysis ---
including the semantic formula, the contrast with propositional \ird{zá-}, and
the derivation of the contrastive implication from exhaustivity --- see
§\,\ref{sec:vm-ident-neg} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs}.

\subsection{Existential clauses: \ird{zad-}}

In existential clauses, the presence of Neg\textsuperscript{0} in the derivation
triggers suppletive root replacement: the existential root \ird{jec-} is
replaced by \ird{zad-}, which carries the full aspect paradigm of a finite verb.
Neg\textsuperscript{0} itself has no independent PF exponent in this
configuration; it is discharged structurally by licensing the suppletive
Vocabulary Item. The prefix \ird{zá-} does not appear because there is no
Neg\textsuperscript{0} head remaining to be spelled out separately. See
§\,\ref{sec:zad} for paradigm and exemplification.

\subsection{Mood-internal negation: prohibitive and negative hortative}

The prohibitive \ird{-é}, the negative hortative \ird{-ku}, and the negative
irrealis \ird{-ir} each carry [+\textsc{neg}] as a lexically specified feature
of the \synhead{MoodP} Vocabulary Item. When a \synhead{MoodP} head bears this
feature, \synhead{NegP} is absent from the derivation: the clause is built with
the negative \synhead{MoodP} merging directly above \synhead{AspP}, and
\ird{zá-} does not surface because there is no Neg\textsuperscript{0} node for
it to spell out. The [+\textsc{neg}] of these forms is directive-scoped
(qualifying a command, suggestion, or hypothetical), not propositional, and
therefore not equivalent to the truth-conditional negation of \synhead{NegP}.
For the full \synhead{MoodP} analysis, including the paradigm of affirmative and
negative Vocabulary Items and the restricted palatalizing behaviour of
\ird{-ir}, see §\,\ref{sec:vm-moodp} of Chapter~\ref{ch:verbs}.

\section{Comparative and superlative constructions}\label{sec:comparison}

Comparison in Iridian is expressed analytically using particles that combine
with stative verbs. The subject of comparison stands in the direct case; the
standard of comparison takes the indirect case. Two particles are
distinguished: \ird{stá} for direct comparison (\textit{more\ldots than},
\textit{-er than}) and \ird{zrc} for equative comparison (\textit{as\ldots
as}).

\subsection{Direct comparison}

Direct comparison is formed with the particle \ird{stá} placed between the
standard of comparison and the stative verb. The stative verb inflects for the
full range of aspects, giving the usual range of meanings --- ongoing state,
entry into the state, past state, and so on --- within the comparative frame:

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek Marcí stá četna.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} Marek.\Ind{} \textsc{comp} be.tall-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is taller than Marek.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek Marcí stá četnime.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} Marek.\Ind{} \textsc{comp} be.tall-\Prog{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is becoming taller than Marek.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek Marcí stá četnik.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} Marek.\Ind{} \textsc{comp} be.tall-\Pf{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek became taller than Marek.}//
\endgl
\xe

\noindent where \ird{četn-} is \trsl{be tall}. The particle \ird{stá} may also
combine with the \ird{-á} attributive form (§\ref{sec:stative-attr}) to form
an attributive comparative. The particle precedes the \ird{-á} form
prenominally:

\pex
\a \ird{stá četná pres} \trsl{the taller man}
\a \ird{Marcí stá četná pres} \trsl{the man who is taller than Marek}
\xe

\subsection{Equative comparison}

Equative comparison is formed with the particle \ird{zrc}. The structure is
otherwise identical to the direct comparative, with the subject in the direct
case and the standard in the indirect. Negation of the equative falls on the
stative verb itself, which takes the regular negative prefix \ird{zá-}
(§\ref{sec:negation}):

\pex
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek Marcí zrc četna.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} Marek.\Ind{} \textsc{eq} be.tall-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is as tall as Marek.}//
\endgl
\a
\begingl
\gla Janek Marcí zrc záčetna.//
\glb Janek.\Dir{} Marek.\Ind{} \textsc{eq} \Neg{}-be.tall-\Cont{}//
\glft \trsl{Janek is not as tall as Marek.}//
\endgl
\xe
