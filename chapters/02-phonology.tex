\chapter{Phonology}\label{ch:phon}

\section{Introduction}

This chapter describes the phonology of standard Iridian as spoken in Roubže and the surrounding region. In its modern form the language shows a distinctly West Slavic profile: a strong opposition between hard and soft consonants, productive front-vowel palatalization, final devoicing and cluster voicing, and a set of morphophonemic stem alternations shaped by the historical loss of weak vowels. The analysis adopted here is synchronic, but the system is best understood as the residue of earlier palatalizing and weak-vowel processes that have since become morphologized in parts of the grammar.

\section{Vowels}

\subsection{Vowel inventory}

Iridian has five short-long vowel pairs: /a\,aː/, /ɛ\,eː/, /ɪ\,iː/, /ɔ\,oː/, /ʊ\,uː/. Long vowels are tenser than their short counterparts except for /a\,aː/.

\subsection{Vowel reduction}

The short front vowels /ɛ/ and /ɪ/ reduce to [ɨ] in unstressed positions. Final /ɛ/ is not reduced before a pause. Unstressed /a/ is centralized to [ɐ], and between soft consonants it may further front.

\subsection{Diphthongs}

Iridian has three phonemic diphthongs: \ird{au}\,/au̯/, \ird{ei}\,/eɪ̯/, and \ird{ou}\,/ou̯/. In older descriptions /eɪ̯/ stands closer to long \ird{é}; in the modern language the contrast is maintained chiefly in careful speech and in morphophonemically conservative items.

\subsection{Lenition and voicing}

The velar stop /k/ is frequently realized as [k͡x] after a pause and lenites to [x] or [ɣ] intervocalically and in certain clusters. This distribution accounts for most surface voiced velars in native vocabulary. Loanwords may preserve [g] in careful pronunciation, but in ordinary speech they are commonly adapted with \orth{h} or with the native lenited series.

Obstruent voicing is phonemic, but clusters obey regressive voicing assimilation, while liquids and nasals do not themselves voice a preceding obstruent. Word-final obstruents are devoiced.

\subsection{Hard and soft consonants}\label{sec:palatalization}

Iridian consonants are divided into hard and soft series. Softness is productively triggered by front-vocalic material, especially suffixes beginning in \ird{i}, \ird{í}, \ird{ě}, and the verbal linker \ird{-e}. The softening is strongest with coronals and velars, but labials and liquids also show secondary palatal articulation.

The exact orthographic reflex depends on the suffix class. Before nominal \ird{-í}, velar /k/ yields \orth{c}, as in \ird{Marek} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Marcí}. Before verbal \ird{-ěv}, the same stem-final velar surfaces as \orth{č}, as in \ird{zděk-} $\rightarrow$ \ird{zdíčev}. This difference is synchronically morphologized: both belong to the broad palatalization system, but individual suffixes select distinct spellout conventions inside that system.

Standard Iridian orthography does not introduce Czech-style haček letters for soft \orth{t}, \orth{d}, or \orth{l}. These consonants may be phonologically soft in front-vocalic environments, but the writing system is conservative and normally represents them with the ordinary base letters unless an established suffix-specific spelling convention applies, as with \orth{c} or \orth{č} after velars.

\section{Phonotactics}

\subsection{Syllable structure}

The basic syllable structure is CV(C). The maximum is CCCCVCCC, though most syllables fall into one of CV, CVC, CCV, CCVC, or CVCC.

\subsection{Stress}

Iridian has initial stress. Stress falls on the first syllable of the phonological word, including prefixes. Clitics are unstressed and form a phonological unit with the following word.

\section{Suprasegmentals}

\subsection{Intonation}

Declaratives are characterized by a falling contour, yes-no questions by a final rise, and focused contrastive phrases by an expanded pitch excursion on the focused constituent. Detailed discourse intonation is treated in Chapter~\ref{ch:discourse}.

\section{Morphophonemic processes}\label{sec:morphophon}

This section describes the stem alternations conditioned by morphology. In the modern language these processes are best analysed in the following order:

\begin{center}
\textsc{suffix selection} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{unstable-vowel deletion} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{vowel raising} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{mutation type selection} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{surface cleanup}
\end{center}

The crucial distinction is between \textbf{sibilant replacement}, a closed morphological mutation class, and \textbf{palatalization}, the productive front-vocalic softening system.

\subsection{Sibilant replacement}\label{sec:sibilant-replacement}

Sibilant replacement is a morphologized stem mutation triggered by a closed set of suffixes, above all the antipassive \ird{-aš-}. It is not ordinary front-vowel palatalization, even when the resulting segment is itself a sibilant. The mutation targets the thematic consonant only and follows the classes in Table~\ref{tab:sibilant-mutation}.

\begin{table}[ht]
	\footnotesize\sffamily
	\caption{Sibilant replacement by thematic class.}\label{tab:sibilant-mutation}
	\medskip
	\begin{tabularx}{0.9\textwidth}{l L L L}
		\toprule
		{\sc class} & {\sc thematic consonants} & {\sc sibilant output} & {\sc example} \\
		\midrule
		I    & -t, -k, -c, -č & → -č   & \ird{piašt-} → \ird{piašč-} \\
		II   & -s, -š         & → -š   & \ird{tras-} → \ird{traš-} \\
		II-A & -l, -p         & + -š   & \ird{dal-} → \ird{dalš-} \\
		III  & -d, -h, -j, -z, -ž & → -ž & \ird{vad-} → \ird{váž-} \\
		III-A & all remaining & + -ž   & \ird{ščen-} → \ird{ščenž-} \\
		\bottomrule
	\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

In Classes I and III the thematic consonant is replaced; in Classes II-A and III-A the sibilant is added after the preserved consonant. Surface [g]-bearing loans, where they participate in this pattern at all, follow the voiced velar/fricative series and are assigned analogically to Class III.

Only true replacement, not mere augmentation, feeds compensatory lengthening in the conservative stratum of the language. This creates a useful structural contrast between replacement classes and additive classes.

\subsection{Unstable-vowel deletion}\label{sec:vowel-zero}

A productive vowel-zero alternation affects roots containing an \textbf{unstable} \ird{e}. This unstable vowel is deleted before vowel-initial suffixes when the resulting consonant cluster is phonotactically acceptable. The core pattern is found in roots of the type -C(\sx{j})eC.

\pex
\a \ird{Janek} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Janka}
\a \ird{obel} $\rightarrow$ \ird{oblu}
\a \ird{pizen} $\rightarrow$ \ird{pězní}, not \ird{\sx{*}pizní}
\xe

Deletion is partly conditioned by cluster shape. In monosyllables, stop-initial roots most readily lose the unstable vowel before a nasal or fricative coda, while fricative-initial roots most readily do so before a nasal. Outside the productive pattern the vowel may be preserved lexically.

\subsection{Vowel raising}\label{sec:vowel-raising}

When unstable-vowel deletion creates a new heavy cluster, the preceding vowel may raise. This is a stem-grade alternation rather than a mere phonetic adjustment. Two correspondence sets are central in the modern language:

\begin{itemize}
	\item front-vowel raising: \ird{e/ě/é} $\rightarrow$ \ird{i/í}
	\item back-vowel raising: \ird{o} $\rightarrow$ \ird{u}
\end{itemize}

The process is productive in a restricted but still synchronically active set of nominal and verbal subclasses.

\pex
\a \ird{lobek} $\rightarrow$ \ird{lubku} \lingcontext{zero-grade plus back-vowel raising}
\a \ird{kostel} $\rightarrow$ \ird{kustlár}
\a \ird{pěštel} $\rightarrow$ \ird{píštlár}
\a \ird{obel} $\rightarrow$ \ird{oblí}, not \ird{\sx{*}obelí} or \ird{\sx{*}ublí}
\xe

Not every zero-grade form raises. The alternation is strongest where deletion affects the final syllable and the preceding syllable remains available as the locus of the grade shift. Some lexemes preserve older grades only in derivation or in a subset of case forms.

\subsection{Palatalization}\label{sec:front-palatalization}

Palatalization is the productive mutation triggered by front-vocalic suffixes. The trigger class includes \ird{i}, \ird{í}, \ird{ě}, and the verbal linker \ird{-e}; it also includes larger suffixes whose first segment is one of these vowels. Palatalization should therefore be treated separately from sibilant replacement.

\pex
\a \ird{Marek} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Marcí} \lingcontext{nominal \ird{-í} trigger}
\a \ird{zděk-} $\rightarrow$ \ird{zdíčev} \lingcontext{verbal \ird{-ěv} trigger}
\a \ird{piašček} $\rightarrow$ \ird{piaščice} \lingcontext{conjunctive \ird{-e} trigger on the final velar of the perfective suffix}
\xe

The conditional converb \ird{-ic} is the chief exception: despite its front vowel, it does not trigger palatalization. This is a historical irregularity preserved in the modern system. Other irregularities in the palatalizing domain are concentrated not in the endings themselves but in suffix-specific spellouts and a handful of high-frequency lexical stems.

\subsection{Compensatory lengthening}\label{sec:comp-lengthening}

When a thematic consonant is replaced, rather than merely augmented, the preceding vowel may lengthen if the replacement would otherwise reduce the moraic weight of the stem. In the modern language this rule remains useful chiefly in older Class~I and Class~III stems under true sibilant replacement. It is therefore not a general repair strategy, but a restricted morphologized contrast between replacement stems and additive stems.

The most conservative pattern is:

\begin{itemize}
	\item replacement classes: short vowel + replaced consonant $\rightarrow$ long vowel + sibilantized output
	\item additive classes: short vowel + preserved consonant $\rightarrow$ no compensatory lengthening
\end{itemize}

Thus a replacement stem may surface with lengthening, while an additive stem in the same derivational environment does not:

\pex
\a \ird{vad-} + \ird{-aš-} $\rightarrow$ \ird{váž-}
\a \ird{dal-} + \ird{-aš-} $\rightarrow$ \ird{dalš-}
\xe

Orthographically the lengthening is written with the regular acute accent. Synchronically this is most productive in a limited conservative lexical stratum and most visible in high-frequency derived verb stems.

\subsection{Historical residue}

The modern morphophonemics contain a small but natural cluster of irregularities. These arise from three sources: analogical leveling, differential preservation of older weak-vowel deletion, and suffix-specific palatalization spellouts. The effect is strongest in high-frequency stems and in clause-linking forms, while the core case endings and the basic sibilant-replacement classes remain comparatively regular.

\subsection{Perfective suffix dissimilation}\label{sec:pf-dissim}

The bare intransitive perfective suffix \ird{-ek} raises to \ird{-ik} when the stem's final syllable has \ird{ě} or \ird{e} as its nucleus, avoiding a CěCe or CeCe sequence across a single intervening consonant. The raising does not apply when a voice morpheme intervenes between the stem and the perfective suffix.

\subsection{Rule interactions}\label{sec:morphophon-interactions}

The alternations above often stack. The following derivations illustrate the intended order.

\pex
\a \ird{piašt-} + \ird{-aš-} + \ird{-ek} $\rightarrow$ \ird{piašček}
\lingcontext{sibilant replacement; no palatalization involved}
\a \ird{zděk-} + \ird{-ěv} $\rightarrow$ \ird{zdíčev}
\lingcontext{front-vocalic trigger; front-vowel raising plus palatalization}
\a \ird{lobek} + \ird{-u} $\rightarrow$ \ird{lubku}
\lingcontext{unstable-vowel deletion feeds back-vowel raising before the case ending}
\a \ird{Marek} + \ird{-í} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Marcí}
\lingcontext{front-vocalic case ending selects the soft stem}
\a \ird{piašček} + \ird{-e} $\rightarrow$ \ird{piaščice}
\lingcontext{conjunctive \ird{-e} palatalizes the final consonant of the inflected stem}
\xe

These derivations are cited throughout Chapters~\ref{ch:verbs}, \ref{ch:nouns}, and~\ref{ch:complex}.
