\chapter{Phonology}\label{ch:phon}

\section{Introduction}

This chapter describes the phonology of standard Iridian as spoken in Roubže and the surrounding region. In its modern form the language shows a distinctly West Slavic profile: a strong opposition between hard and soft consonants, productive front-vowel palatalization, final devoicing and cluster voicing, and a dense set of morphophonemic stem alternations shaped above all by the highly productive behavior of weak \ird{e}. The analysis adopted here is synchronic, but the system is best understood as the residue of earlier palatalizing and weak-vowel processes that have been generalized anew in the modern language.

\section{Vowels}

\subsection{Vowel inventory}

Iridian has six short-long vowel pairs in careful standard speech: /a\,aː/, /ɛ\,eː/, /ɪ\,iː/, /ɨ\,ɨː/, /ɔ\,oː/, /ʊ\,uː/. Long vowels are tenser than their short counterparts except for /a\,aː/. Orthographic \orth{i, í} represent the palatalizing front series /ɪ,\,iː/, while \orth{y, ý} represent the non-palatalizing central series /ɨ,\,ɨː/.

\subsection{Vowel reduction}

The short vowels /ɛ/ and /ɪ/ reduce toward centralized [ɨ]-like qualities in unstressed positions, and in rapid speech this can partially overlap with the ordinary phonetic range of stressed \ird{y}. Final /ɛ/ is not reduced before a pause. Unstressed /a/ is centralized to [ɐ], and between soft consonants it may further front.

\subsection{Diphthongs}

Iridian has three phonemic diphthongs: \ird{au}\,/au̯/, \ird{ei}\,/eɪ̯/, and \ird{ou}\,/ou̯/. In older descriptions /eɪ̯/ stands closer to long \ird{é}; in the modern language the contrast is maintained chiefly in careful speech and in morphophonemically conservative items.

The palatal nasal /ň/ is a full member of the consonant inventory, not merely an allophonic reflex of /n/. It occurs most robustly before front vowels and in morphologically soft stems, but in the modern standard it is best treated phonemically. By contrast, /g/ is not a native phoneme: any surface [g] belongs either to careful loan pronunciation or to a narrow phonetic realization of the native velar series.

\subsection{Lenition and voicing}

The velar stop /k/ is frequently realized as [k͡x] after a pause and lenites to [x] or [ɣ] intervocalically and in certain clusters. This distribution accounts for most surface voiced velars in native vocabulary. Loanwords may preserve [g] in careful pronunciation, but in ordinary speech they are commonly adapted with \orth{h} or with the native lenited series.

Obstruent voicing is phonemic, but clusters obey regressive voicing assimilation, while liquids and nasals do not themselves voice a preceding obstruent. Word-final obstruents are devoiced. The same voicing rules apply across morpheme boundaries and therefore form part of the regular morphophonemic surface cleanup described below.

\subsection{Hard and soft consonants}\label{sec:palatalization}

Iridian consonants are divided into hard and soft series. Softness is productively triggered by front-vocalic material, especially suffixes beginning in \ird{i}, \ird{í}, \ird{ě}, and the verbal linker \ird{-e}. The softening is strongest with coronals and velars, but labials and liquids also show secondary palatal articulation.

\subsection{Orthographic reflexes of softness}

The exact orthographic reflex of softness depends on the suffix class as well as on the consonant involved. Before nominal \ird{-í}, velar /k/ yields \orth{c}, as in \ird{Marek} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Marcí}. Before verbal \ird{-ěv}, the same stem-final velar surfaces as \orth{č}, as in \ird{zděk-} $\rightarrow$ \ird{zdíčev}. This difference is synchronically morphologized: both belong to the broad palatalization system, but individual suffixes select distinct spellout conventions inside that system.

For velars, the most revealing analysis is one of \term{front-vocalic affrication}. Under nominal front-vocalic morphology, /k/ becomes the alveolar affricate /c/, while in stronger verbal palatalizing environments it surfaces as /č/. The alternation is therefore not merely graphic. It is a genuine morphophonemic shift of the velar series into an affricated fronted output. Since /g/ is absent from the native phoneme inventory, the productive alternation is built around /k/ and /h/, not around a voiced velar stop pair.

Standard Iridian orthography does not introduce Czech-style haček letters for soft \orth{t}, \orth{d}, or \orth{l}. These consonants may be phonologically soft in front-vocalic environments, but the writing system is conservative and normally represents them with the ordinary base letters unless an established suffix-specific spelling convention applies, as with \orth{c} or \orth{č} after velars. The practical result is that the orthography is somewhat less explicit than the phonology: case endings and verbal suffixes often reveal softness only indirectly, through a preceding vowel letter or through a suffix-specific spelling convention.

\section{Phonotactics}

\subsection{Syllable structure}

The basic syllable structure is CV(C). The maximum is CCCCVCCC, though most syllables fall into one of CV, CVC, CCV, CCVC, or CVCC.

\subsection{Syllabic liquids}

Like its West Slavic neighbors, Iridian permits liquids to bear a syllable in reduced environments. The rhotic and the lateral may surface as syllabic [r̩] and [l̩] when weak \ird{e} is deleted and the language chooses not to restore a vowel. This is especially natural at morpheme boundaries and in fast speech, though orthography remains conservative and normally writes only the consonantal letters.

Syllabic liquids are not a free alternative to ordinary vocalism. They appear chiefly when three conditions are met: the deleted vowel is weak \ird{e}; the resulting cluster already contains a liquid; and no simpler repair such as straightforward cluster retention is available. In this way the language accommodates dense consonant groupings without abandoning its preference for preserving inherited stem shapes.

\subsection{Stress}

Iridian has initial stress. Stress falls on the first syllable of the phonological word, including prefixes. Clitics are unstressed and form a phonological unit with the following word.

\section{Suprasegmentals}

\subsection{Intonation}

Declaratives are characterized by a falling contour, yes-no questions by a final rise, and focused contrastive phrases by an expanded pitch excursion on the focused constituent. Detailed discourse intonation is treated in Chapter~\ref{ch:discourse}.

\section{Morphophonemic processes}\label{sec:morphophon}

Iridian morphology is surface-rich but structurally regular. The same rule system applies across noun inflection, verb derivation, converbs, clause-linking forms, and nominalizations, though nouns expose it most densely. In the modern language the morphophonemic processes are best analysed in the following order:

\begin{center}
\textsc{suffix class selection} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{weak-e deletion or retention} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{grade formation} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{soft-stem selection / palatalization} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{cluster repair / epenthesis} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{voicing cleanup} $\rightarrow$ \textsc{analogical leveling}
\end{center}

The ordering matters. A noun such as \ird{lobek} first selects the transitive ending of its declension class, then deletes weak \ird{e}, then raises \ird{o} to \ird{u}, and only after that reaches its final cluster shape. A noun of the type \ird{perek} first deletes weak \ird{e}, then forms the liquid cluster \ird{prk-}, and only then affricates the velar before a front-vocalic indirect ending, yielding \ird{prce}. A verb such as \ird{zděk-} behaves the same way before a palatalizing suffix, except that the palatalized grade rather than a nominal oblique stem is the output.

\subsection{Suffix classes and their triggers}

The most useful synchronic generalization is that suffixes belong to morphophonemic trigger classes. A suffix does not merely add segmental material; it also selects a particular type of stem.

The language is therefore not best described as having a small number of isolated alternations. Rather, it has a network of suffix classes that repeatedly select zero-grade, raised, soft, or leveled stems.

\subsection{Vowel-replacing nominal endings}

Not all nominal alternations are driven by deletion alone. A small but productive set of noun classes selects endings that replace a final stem vowel rather than simply attaching to it. This is most visible in inherited \ird{-o} stems, where the transitive replaces the final vowel with \ird{-a} and the indirect adds a nasal element that coalesces with the preceding back vowel:

\pex
\a \ird{bro} + \ird{-a} $\rightarrow$ \ird{bra}
\a \ird{bro} + \ird{-m} $\rightarrow$ \ird{brum}
\xe

Synchronically the useful generalization is \ird{o + m > um}. The rule belongs to the noun system rather than to the verbal domain and should be treated as a class-specific back-vocalic contraction.

\subsection{Weak \ird{e} and zero-grade formation}\label{sec:vowel-zero}

The central morphophonemic process of modern Iridian is the behavior of \term{weak} \ird{e}. Synchronically this vowel is not confined to a few fossilized stems. It remains highly productive in nouns and still active, though less pervasive, in verbs and derived forms. Weak \ird{e} is deleted before many vowel-initial and front-vocalic suffixes whenever the resulting cluster is phonotactically acceptable.

The core environment is a final syllable of the shape -C(\sx{j})eC, especially before case endings and other suffixes that begin with a vowel. In structural terms the process is comparable to the West Slavic type illustrated by \foreign{pes} $\rightarrow$ \foreign{psa}: a weak mid vowel in the final stem syllable is lost when a vowel-initial oblique ending follows and the resulting consonant sequence is acceptable.

\pex
\a \ird{pes} + \ird{-a} $\rightarrow$ \ird{psa}
\a \ird{Janek} + \ird{-u} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Janku}
\a \ird{perek} + \ird{-u} $\rightarrow$ \ird{prku}
\a \ird{obel} + \ird{-u} $\rightarrow$ \ird{oblu}
\a \ird{Merek} + \ird{-í} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Mercí}
\xe

Deletion applies most readily when the resulting cluster belongs to one of the cluster shapes already tolerated by Iridian phonotactics: obstruent + sonorant, sibilant + obstruent, sonorant + obstruent, or a cluster that may be resolved with syllabic \ird{r} or \ird{l}. The process is therefore strong but not blind. Iridian allows heavy clustering, yet it does not permit every possible sequence to arise unchecked at a morpheme boundary.

Deletion is blocked in exactly three circumstances. First, some declension classes and some individual stems are lexically marked as non-deleting. Second, deletion is blocked when it would create an illicit cluster, above all a dense all-obstruent cluster without a licensed sibilant edge or liquid nucleus. Third, a small set of high-frequency forms preserves an analogically levelled vowel despite the general rule.

The productivity of weak-\ird{e} deletion is greatest in noun oblique formation. It is especially common in consonant-final declension classes, in velar stems, and in the \ird{-a/-am} family. A particularly important subtype is the \term{CeLeC} pattern, where deletion yields a stable \ird{CLC-} oblique stem and the class-specific case vowel then determines the final output: \ird{perek} $\rightarrow$ \ird{prku} in the transitive, \ird{prce} in the front-vocalic indirect, or \ird{prkam} in the \ird{-am} family. In verbal morphology the process is most visible before the irrealis, the simultaneous converb, the linker \ird{-e}, and selected nominalizers:

\pex
\a \ird{zděk-} + \ird{-ěv} $\rightarrow$ \ird{zdíčev}
\a \ird{perek} + \ird{-ěc} $\rightarrow$ \ird{prčec}
\a \ird{obel} + \ird{-ou} $\rightarrow$ \ird{oblou}
\xe

\subsection{Grade formation and vowel raising}\label{sec:vowel-raising}

When weak-\ird{e} deletion creates a heavy cluster, the stem may enter a raised grade. This is a morphologized grade alternation, not a mere phonetic tendency. Two productive correspondences dominate the modern language:

\begin{itemize}
	\item front-vowel raising: \ird{e/ě/é} $\rightarrow$ \ird{i/í}
	\item back-vowel raising: \ird{o} $\rightarrow$ \ird{u}
\end{itemize}

In the noun system the raised grade is lexically tied to particular declension classes. In the verb system it is much rarer, but still productive before a narrow set of front-vocalic suffixes and in some nominalizing formations. The rule is therefore general, even though its frequency differs sharply by domain. Because several noun classes have undergone areal reshaping under prolonged Slavic contact, raising may coexist with newer analogical endings such as \ird{-ení} or \ird{-ovi}; the stem grade and the case ending must therefore be treated as partially independent layers.

\pex
\a \ird{lobek} + \ird{-u} $\rightarrow$ \ird{lubku} \lingcontext{back-vowel raising after weak-\ird{e} deletion}
\a \ird{pěštel} + \ird{-í} $\rightarrow$ \ird{píštlí} \lingcontext{front-vowel raising in a noun oblique}
\a \ird{zděk-} + \ird{-ěv} $\rightarrow$ \ird{zdíčev} \lingcontext{front-vowel raising in the verbal domain}
\xe

Not every zero-grade form raises. Whether raising applies is determined first by declension class or stem class, and only secondarily by phonological environment. The important synchronic generalization is not ``delete weak \ird{e}, then optionally raise'' but ``some classes select a raised oblique stem once weak \ird{e} has been lost''.

\subsection{Soft-stem selection and palatalization}\label{sec:front-palatalization}

Palatalization is the productive mutation triggered by front-vocalic suffixes. The trigger class includes \ird{i}, \ird{í}, \ird{ě}, the linker \ird{-e}, and larger suffixes whose first segment is one of these vowels. Front-vocalic morphology therefore selects a \term{soft stem}, whether in the noun system or in the verbal domain. In the noun system this soft stem may then combine either with inherited short endings such as \ird{-í} or with expanded endings such as \ird{-ení}; both are synchronically members of the front-vocalic oblique domain.

The strongest outputs occur with coronals and velars. Velars have the most visible orthographic alternations, while other consonants may be soft only phonologically or through conservative spelling conventions.

\pex
\a \ird{Marek} + \ird{-í} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Marcí} \lingcontext{nominal soft stem with velar affrication}
\a \ird{perek} + \ird{-e} $\rightarrow$ \ird{prce} \lingcontext{CeLeC stem plus velar affrication}
\a \ird{ruk} + \ird{-ě} $\rightarrow$ \ird{ruce} \lingcontext{front-vocalic indirect ending}
\a \ird{zděk-} + \ird{-ěv} $\rightarrow$ \ird{zdíčev} \lingcontext{verbal soft stem}
\xe

Soft-stem selection comes after zero-grade and grade formation. If a noun both deletes weak \ird{e} and takes a front-vocalic ending, the language first constructs the zero or raised stem and only then applies softening:

\pex
\a \ird{lobek} + \ird{-í} $\rightarrow$ \ird{lubcí}
\a \ird{Janek} + \ird{-í} $\rightarrow$ \ird{Jancí}
\a \ird{perek} + \ird{-e} $\rightarrow$ \ird{prce}
\xe

The conditional converb \ird{-ic} remains the chief exception: despite its front vowel, it does not trigger palatalization. This is a lexicalized historical irregularity preserved in the modern language.

\subsection{Sibilant replacement}\label{sec:sibilant-replacement}

Sibilant replacement is a closed morphologized mutation class triggered chiefly by the antipassive \ird{-aš-}. It is not ordinary front-vowel palatalization, even when the resulting segment is itself a sibilant. The mutation targets the thematic consonant only and follows the classes in Table~\ref{tab:sibilant-mutation}.

Surface [g]-bearing loans, where they participate in this pattern at all, follow the voiced velar/fricative series and are assigned analogically to Class III. Once replacement has applied, later softening or voicing cleanup may still affect the output if the following suffix requires it.

\subsection{Cluster repair, epenthesis, and voicing cleanup}

The productivity of weak-\ird{e} deletion would produce unwieldy strings if it were not paired with strong surface-repair rules. Iridian is permissive about clustering by European standards, but it still regularizes derived clusters in a principled way. The language prefers to keep the full consonant sequence when possible, then to exploit syllabic liquids, then to simplify or assimilate, and only last of all to restore a vowel. This ordering gives the surface a recognizably West Slavic density without making every derived cluster equally acceptable.

\begin{table}[ht]
	\footnotesize\sffamily
	\caption{Regularization hierarchy at morpheme boundaries.}\label{tab:cluster-repair}
	\medskip
	\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{l L L}
		\toprule
		{\sc rank} & {\sc strategy} & {\sc effect} \\
		\midrule
		1 & retain the cluster & keep the result of deletion if the cluster is otherwise licit \\
		2 & syllabic liquid formation & realize \ird{r} or \ird{l} as [r̩] or [l̩] in liquid-bearing clusters \\
		3 & assimilation or simplification & spread voicing, soften leftward locally, or delete the weakest coronal in an illicit all-obstruent group \\
		4 & epenthesis / vowel retention & restore a short vowel only when no consonantal repair yields a well-formed output \\
		\bottomrule
	\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

Four repairs are central in the modern language.

First, obstruent clusters obey regressive voicing assimilation, including across morpheme boundaries:

\pex
\a \ird{obez + -ba} $\rightarrow$ \ird{obezba}, phonetically [obezba]
\a \ird{otk + -da} $\rightarrow$ [ogda]
\a \ird{vrad + -ka} $\rightarrow$ [vratka]
\xe

Second, word-final obstruents are devoiced regardless of the underlying consonant or the derivational history of the word.

Third, when deletion produces a cluster containing a liquid, Iridian often preserves the deletion and allows the liquid to become syllabic rather than restoring the vowel:

\pex
\a \ird{berel} + \ird{-a} $\rightarrow$ [br̩la] or orthographic \ird{brla}
\a \ird{perek} + \ird{-am} $\rightarrow$ [pr̩kam] in the most reduced liquid-bearing type
\xe

Fourth, when deletion yields an illicit all-obstruent cluster, the language repairs it by assimilation or simplification before resorting to epenthesis. The usual simplification is loss of the weakest coronal obstruent or merger into a neighboring sibilant; the usual assimilation is regressive voicing and, more locally, place or softness assimilation.

Softness may also spread leftward one segment inside a cluster, especially when a coronal directly precedes a newly soft consonant. This spread is limited and not always reflected orthographically.

If no consonantal repair succeeds, a short epenthetic vowel is inserted or the weak vowel is retained. Epenthesis is therefore a last resort, not a general stem-building strategy. This is the main reason Iridian can remain generous with clusters while still avoiding complete surface chaos at morpheme boundaries.

\subsection{Compensatory lengthening}\label{sec:comp-lengthening}

When a thematic consonant is replaced, rather than merely augmented, the preceding vowel may lengthen if the replacement would otherwise reduce the moraic weight of the stem. In the modern language this rule remains useful chiefly in older Class~I and Class~III verbal stems under true sibilant replacement. It is therefore not a general repair strategy, but a restricted morphologized contrast between replacement stems and additive stems.

The most conservative pattern is:

\begin{itemize}
	\item replacement classes: short vowel + replaced consonant $\rightarrow$ long vowel + sibilantized output
	\item additive classes: short vowel + preserved consonant $\rightarrow$ no compensatory lengthening
\end{itemize}

\pex
\a \ird{vad-} + \ird{-aš-} $\rightarrow$ \ird{váž-}
\a \ird{dal-} + \ird{-aš-} $\rightarrow$ \ird{dalš-}
\xe

Orthographically the lengthening is written with the regular acute accent. Synchronically this is most visible in high-frequency derived verb stems and in a conservative lexical stratum.

\subsection{Orthographic reflexes of morphophonemic alternations}

The orthography represents the major alternations, but unevenly. Raised vowels are written straightforwardly. Velar palatalization is regularly visible as \orth{c} or \orth{č}, depending on suffix class. By contrast, soft coronals and liquids often remain spelled with their base letters. The result is a conservative writing system with a more dynamic spoken stem alternation pattern underneath it.

Weak-\ird{e} deletion is usually not marked directly, since the deleted vowel simply fails to appear in the oblique or derived form. Thus \ird{obel} : \ird{oblu} and \ird{Janek} : \ird{Janku} are written exactly as they are pronounced. The writing system is therefore conservative chiefly in the domain of softness, not in the domain of deletion or raising.

\subsection{Analogical leveling and historical residue}

The modern morphophonemics contain a natural but limited cluster of irregularities. These arise from three sources: analogical leveling, differential preservation of weak-\ird{e} deletion, and suffix-specific palatalization spellouts. A fourth source now deserves separate mention: areal reshaping. Several noun classes have generalized longer oblique endings such as \ird{-ení} and animate-like endings such as \ird{-ovi} under prolonged contact pressure from neighboring Slavic systems. The strongest irregularity is therefore concentrated in high-frequency nouns, animate lexemes, clause-linking forms, and a few older verbal derivatives. The core noun case system nevertheless remains rule-driven once the declension class of the lexeme is known.

\subsection{Perfective suffix dissimilation}\label{sec:pf-dissim}

The bare intransitive perfective suffix \ird{-ek} raises to \ird{-ik} when the stem's final syllable has \ird{ě} or \ird{e} as its nucleus, avoiding a CěCe or CeCe sequence across a single intervening consonant. The raising does not apply when a voice morpheme intervenes between the stem and the perfective suffix. This alternation belongs to the same general tendency toward grade optimization in front-vocalic environments, but is more narrowly delimited than nounal raising.
